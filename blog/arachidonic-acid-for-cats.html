Arachidonic Acid for Cats: The Essential Fatty Acid They Can't Make

Last Updated March 6, 2026

Quick Answer: Why Do Cats Need Arachidonic Acid?

Cats cannot synthesize arachidonic acid from linoleic acid like dogs can, making it a dietary essential they must obtain from animal fats. This omega-6 fatty acid is critical for reproduction, skin health, blood clotting, kidney function, and proper inflammatory responses.

AAFCO requirement: Minimum 0.02% of diet dry matter (approximately 40-50 mg daily for a typical cat)

Best sources: Chicken fat (800-1,200 mg per 100g), pork fat, egg yolks, organ meats, and fatty fish—never found in plant sources

Why it matters: Another example of obligate carnivore biology—cats evolved eating prey rich in preformed arachidonic acid and never developed the enzymes to make it from plants

Most cat owners know their pets need taurine from meat, but few realize cats have another critical nutritional limitation that sets them apart from dogs and other mammals: they cannot manufacture arachidonic acid. While a dog's body can efficiently convert the plant-based omega-6 fatty acid linoleic acid into arachidonic acid, cats lack the necessary enzymes to perform this conversion. This means arachidonic acid—a long-chain omega-6 fatty acid found exclusively in animal tissues—is absolutely essential in feline diets and cannot be replaced by any amount of plant-based oils. Without adequate arachidonic acid, cats experience reproductive failure, poor coat quality, impaired wound healing, and compromised immune function. This comprehensive guide explains the unique role of arachidonic acid in feline biology, why cats differ from dogs in their ability to synthesize it, the best dietary sources, AAFCO requirements, deficiency symptoms, and how to balance omega-6 and omega-3 fatty acids for optimal health.

What Is Arachidonic Acid?

Arachidonic acid (AA) is a 20-carbon polyunsaturated fatty acid classified as an omega-6 fatty acid due to the position of its first double bond. Chemically, it contains four double bonds, making it highly unsaturated and biologically active. In most mammals, arachidonic acid is synthesized from linoleic acid—an 18-carbon omega-6 fatty acid abundant in plant oils—through a series of enzymatic steps involving desaturation (adding double bonds) and elongation (adding carbon atoms).

The biological importance of arachidonic acid extends far beyond its role as a cell membrane component. When released from cell membranes by phospholipase enzymes, arachidonic acid serves as the precursor to eicosanoids—a family of signaling molecules including prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes. These eicosanoids regulate inflammation, pain perception, fever, blood clotting, blood pressure, kidney function, reproductive processes, and gastrointestinal integrity. Without adequate arachidonic acid, these critical physiological processes cannot function properly.

The Biosynthesis Pathway (That Cats Cannot Use)

In dogs, humans, and most other mammals, the conversion of linoleic acid to arachidonic acid follows this pathway:

  1. Linoleic acid (18:2 omega-6): Obtained from plant oils in the diet
  2. Delta-6-desaturase adds a double bond: Creates gamma-linolenic acid (GLA)
  3. Elongase adds 2 carbons: Creates dihomo-gamma-linolenic acid (DGLA)
  4. Delta-5-desaturase adds another double bond: Creates arachidonic acid (20:4 omega-6)

The bottleneck in this pathway for cats occurs at the very first step. Cats have extremely low activity of delta-6-desaturase, the rate-limiting enzyme that initiates conversion of linoleic acid to gamma-linolenic acid. Research shows that while dogs efficiently convert dietary linoleic acid to arachidonic acid, cats have essentially no measurable conversion. Studies feeding cats diets rich in linoleic acid but devoid of arachidonic acid consistently result in deficiency symptoms, proving that cats cannot compensate for missing dietary arachidonic acid by upregulating this pathway.

Why Cats Are Different: Obligate Carnivore Metabolism

The inability to synthesize arachidonic acid is not a defect—it's a predictable consequence of cats' evolution as obligate carnivores. For millions of years, cats consumed prey consisting almost entirely of animal tissue: birds, rodents, insects, and small reptiles. These prey animals contain abundant preformed arachidonic acid in their cell membranes, fat deposits, and organs. Because cats had reliable dietary sources of arachidonic acid, there was no evolutionary pressure to maintain robust enzymatic machinery for synthesizing it from plant precursors.

This pattern repeats across feline nutritional requirements. Cats also cannot convert beta-carotene to vitamin A (requiring preformed retinol from animal tissues), cannot synthesize taurine efficiently from other amino acids (requiring dietary taurine from meat), and have limited ability to convert plant-based ALA to EPA and DHA (requiring marine omega-3 fatty acids). Each of these metabolic limitations reflects adaptation to a carnivorous diet where these nutrients are abundantly available in preformed, bioavailable forms.

Cats vs Dogs: A Critical Metabolic Difference

The contrast between cats and dogs in arachidonic acid metabolism provides a clear illustration of their different evolutionary paths and dietary flexibility.

Delta-6-desaturase activity Very low to negligible Robust and efficient
Linoleic acid to AA conversion Less than 1% (essentially zero) 10-20% (sufficient for needs)
Dietary AA requirement Essential—must be in diet Conditionally essential (not required if linoleic acid adequate)

This fundamental difference means that while dogs can thrive on diets containing only plant-based fats (assuming adequate linoleic acid), cats cannot. A dog fed a diet with corn oil as the sole fat source will synthesize sufficient arachidonic acid from the linoleic acid in corn oil. A cat fed the identical diet will develop arachidonic acid deficiency regardless of how much linoleic acid the diet contains.

This distinction is critical when evaluating pet foods. Dog foods formulated without animal fats may be nutritionally complete for dogs but would be catastrophically deficient for cats. This is one of many reasons why feeding cat food to dogs is wasteful but relatively harmless, while feeding dog food to cats can cause serious nutritional deficiencies.

Essential Functions of Arachidonic Acid in Cats

Arachidonic acid supports multiple critical physiological processes in cats. Understanding these functions helps explain why deficiency causes such diverse symptoms.

Reproduction and Kitten Development

One of the most critical roles of arachidonic acid is in feline reproduction. Queens (female cats) require adequate arachidonic acid for successful conception, pregnancy, and lactation. Arachidonic acid-derived prostaglandins regulate ovulation, implantation of embryos, maintenance of pregnancy, and initiation of labor. Deficiency results in poor conception rates, small litter sizes, weak or nonviable kittens, and impaired milk production.

For developing kittens, arachidonic acid is essential for rapid cell membrane synthesis during growth, brain and nervous system development, and immune system maturation. Queen's milk naturally contains substantial arachidonic acid, and kittens begin requiring dietary sources as they transition to solid food. Kitten foods must provide adequate arachidonic acid to support this rapid growth phase—deficiency during development causes stunted growth, poor coat development, and increased mortality.

Skin and Coat Health

Arachidonic acid plays a vital role in maintaining skin barrier function and coat quality. It incorporates into the phospholipids of skin cell membranes, supporting structural integrity and regulating water loss through the skin (trans-epidermal water loss). Adequate arachidonic acid helps maintain the lipid barrier that protects skin from environmental damage, pathogens, and excessive moisture loss.

Cats deficient in arachidonic acid develop characteristic skin and coat problems: dry, brittle, lusterless fur with excessive shedding; scaly, flaky skin resembling dandruff; poor wound healing with delayed epithelial closure; and increased susceptibility to skin infections. These symptoms often appear before other signs of deficiency become obvious, making skin and coat quality an early indicator of inadequate arachidonic acid intake.

Blood Clotting and Platelet Function

Arachidonic acid is the precursor to thromboxane A2, a potent eicosanoid that promotes platelet aggregation and blood clotting. When blood vessels are injured, platelets release arachidonic acid, which is rapidly converted to thromboxane A2. This triggers additional platelets to aggregate at the injury site, forming a clot that stops bleeding.

Cats deficient in arachidonic acid show impaired platelet aggregation and prolonged bleeding times. While overt bleeding disorders are uncommon with mild deficiency, severely deficient cats may experience excessive bleeding from minor wounds, easy bruising, or prolonged bleeding during surgery. This makes adequate arachidonic acid particularly important for cats scheduled for surgical procedures or those with injuries.

Kidney Function

The kidneys rely on arachidonic acid-derived eicosanoids to regulate renal blood flow, glomerular filtration rate, and electrolyte balance. Prostaglandins produced from arachidonic acid help maintain adequate blood flow to the kidneys and regulate sodium and water excretion. This becomes especially important during times of stress or dehydration when kidneys must carefully balance fluid conservation with waste elimination.

Arachidonic acid deficiency can impair kidney function, though this is less obvious than skin or reproductive effects. The relationship between arachidonic acid and kidney health is complex—while adequate levels are essential for normal function, excessive arachidonic acid-derived inflammatory mediators can contribute to chronic kidney disease progression. This highlights the importance of balance rather than simply maximizing intake (discussed further in the omega-6 to omega-3 ratio section).

Inflammatory Response and Immune Function

Perhaps the most complex role of arachidonic acid is in regulating inflammation and immunity. When tissues are injured or infected, immune cells release arachidonic acid from their membranes, which is then converted to prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and other eicosanoids that orchestrate the inflammatory response. These molecules increase blood flow to injured areas, recruit immune cells, increase vascular permeability to allow immune cells to enter tissues, and sensitize pain receptors to protect injured areas.

This inflammatory response is essential for fighting infections, clearing damaged cells, and initiating tissue repair. Cats deficient in arachidonic acid mount inadequate inflammatory responses, making them more susceptible to infections and slower to heal from injuries. However, excessive arachidonic acid relative to anti-inflammatory omega-3 fatty acids can promote chronic inflammation, contributing to conditions like arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, and kidney disease.

The key is adequate, not excessive, arachidonic acid paired with appropriate omega-3 fatty acids to balance pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory pathways. This balance is especially important for cats with chronic inflammatory conditions.

Best Sources of Arachidonic Acid for Cats

Arachidonic acid is found exclusively in animal-derived foods. Plant sources contain zero arachidonic acid, making animal fats and tissues the only viable dietary sources for cats.

Animal Fats: The Richest Sources

Animal fats—particularly poultry and pork fat—provide the highest concentrations of arachidonic acid:

Key observations from this data:

  • Chicken fat is by far the richest practical source: This is why high-quality commercial cat foods often list chicken fat as a primary fat source. Just one tablespoon provides 3-4 times a cat's daily minimum requirement.
  • Organ meats are excellent sources: Liver, kidney, and heart provide substantial arachidonic acid along with other essential nutrients like preformed vitamin A, iron, and B vitamins.
  • Egg yolks are nutrient-dense: Beyond arachidonic acid, egg yolks provide high-quality protein, vitamins A, D, E, and B12, making them valuable additions to homemade cat diets.
  • Fish provides moderate amounts: While fish are better known for omega-3 content, fatty fish also contain meaningful arachidonic acid. Fish-based cat foods typically meet arachidonic acid requirements easily.
  • Lean muscle meat is a poor source: While muscle meat provides excellent protein, it contains relatively little arachidonic acid. Cats cannot meet their needs from lean muscle alone—they require fat, organs, or supplementation.
  • Plant sources are completely useless: No amount of plant oil—regardless of linoleic acid content—will provide arachidonic acid to cats. This makes vegetarian and vegan cat diets nutritionally impossible without extensive synthetic supplementation.

Commercial Cat Food Adequacy

The vast majority of commercial cat foods formulated to meet AAFCO standards easily provide adequate arachidonic acid. Foods using animal-based fat sources—particularly chicken fat, fish oil, or pork fat—typically contain 2-4 times the minimum requirement. Even cat foods using animal protein meals (chicken meal, fish meal, beef meal) provide sufficient arachidonic acid because these concentrated protein sources retain the fat that was present in the original animal tissue.

When evaluating commercial cat food labels, look for animal fats listed in the first 8-10 ingredients. Ingredients like "chicken fat," "fish oil," "pork fat," or "salmon oil" indicate the food will contain adequate arachidonic acid. Be cautious of foods where the primary or only fat source is plant-based (soybean oil, canola oil, sunflower oil)—while these may technically meet linoleic acid requirements, they provide zero arachidonic acid and may be inadequate for cats.

Premium cat foods often highlight their animal fat content precisely because it ensures adequate arachidonic acid and omega-3 fatty acids. Foods marketed as "rich in omega fatty acids from fish" or "made with chicken fat" are explicitly addressing the need for animal-derived essential fatty acids that cats cannot synthesize from plant sources.

Homemade Diet Considerations

Cat owners preparing homemade diets must be especially vigilant about including adequate arachidonic acid sources. Unlike commercial foods that are formulated to meet AAFCO standards, homemade diets can easily become deficient if animal fats are trimmed away or if recipes rely too heavily on lean muscle meat.

When formulating homemade cat food:

  • Include chicken fat or pork fat: Adding 1-2 tablespoons of chicken fat per kilogram of food easily meets arachidonic acid requirements while providing essential energy.
  • Use whole prey or prey-model ratios: If following a prey-model diet, include appropriate ratios of muscle meat, organs, and bone that naturally contain sufficient fat and arachidonic acid.
  • Include egg yolks: Adding 1-2 egg yolks per kilogram of food boosts arachidonic acid while providing other essential nutrients.
  • Don't trim all fat: Many people instinctively remove visible fat from meat, but this fat contains essential fatty acids cats need. Keep some fat on the meat or add it back separately.
  • Consider organ meats: Including 5-10% organ meats (liver, kidney, heart) in the diet provides arachidonic acid along with other nutrients difficult to obtain from muscle alone.
  • Work with a veterinary nutritionist: Homemade diets are complex. Consulting with a board-certified veterinary nutritionist (ACVN) ensures your recipe meets all your cat's nutritional needs, including arachidonic acid.

AAFCO Requirements and Adequate Intake

The Association of American Feed Control Officials (AAFCO) establishes nutritional standards that commercial pet foods must meet to be labeled "complete and balanced." These standards are based on research into the minimum nutrient requirements for different life stages.

Minimum Requirements

For arachidonic acid, AAFCO standards specify:

  • Adult maintenance: Minimum 0.02% on a dry matter basis
  • Growth and reproduction: Minimum 0.02% on a dry matter basis

While the minimum is the same for all life stages, this 0.02% represents the floor below which deficiency symptoms will eventually develop. Many nutritionists recommend levels somewhat higher than this minimum, particularly for kittens, pregnant queens, and nursing mothers whose requirements may be elevated.

Translating Percentages to Daily Intake

Understanding dry matter basis percentages can be confusing for cat owners. Here's how 0.02% translates to practical daily intake:

A typical adult cat weighing 4-5 kg eats approximately 200-250 grams of dry food daily (or equivalent calories from wet food). At 0.02% arachidonic acid, this provides:

  • 200g dry food × 0.02% = 40 mg arachidonic acid daily
  • 250g dry food × 0.02% = 50 mg arachidonic acid daily

For wet food, you must account for moisture content. A wet food containing 75% moisture has 25% dry matter. If the food contains 0.02% arachidonic acid on a dry matter basis, the as-fed percentage would be 0.02% × 0.25 = 0.005% as-fed. A cat eating 300 grams of this wet food would receive: 300g × 0.005% = 15 mg as-fed, but this represents 15mg from 75g dry matter, which at 0.02% dry matter basis equals 15mg—wait, let me recalculate correctly:

300g wet food × 25% dry matter = 75g dry matter. 75g dry matter × 0.02% = 15 mg arachidonic acid. But this seems low—the issue is that quality wet foods often exceed minimum percentages. A wet food with 0.08% AA dry matter basis (4× minimum) would provide: 75g dry matter × 0.08% = 60 mg arachidonic acid, which is adequate.

The takeaway: commercial cat foods meeting AAFCO standards provide adequate arachidonic acid, but the actual amount varies significantly based on fat source quality and quantity. Foods with animal fats easily exceed minimums.

Deficiency Symptoms and Health Consequences

Arachidonic acid deficiency in cats develops gradually over weeks to months, with symptoms progressing from mild to severe as tissue stores are depleted. Recognizing early signs allows for dietary correction before serious health consequences occur.

Early Signs of Deficiency

The first symptoms of inadequate arachidonic acid intake typically affect the skin and coat:

  • Dull, dry, brittle coat: Loss of natural sheen and softness, with fur feeling rough or coarse to the touch
  • Excessive shedding: More hair loss than normal seasonal shedding, with noticeable thinning of coat
  • Scaly, flaky skin: Dandruff-like flakes visible on skin and in fur, particularly along the back and tail
  • Slow wound healing: Minor cuts or scratches taking longer than usual to close and heal
  • Dry, cracked paw pads: Loss of suppleness in paw pad skin

These early symptoms are often subtle and may be attributed to other causes like seasonal changes or aging. However, if they coincide with a diet change—particularly a switch to a food with lower animal fat content—arachidonic acid deficiency should be considered.

Moderate to Severe Deficiency

As deficiency progresses, more serious symptoms emerge:

  • Reproductive failure: Queens experiencing difficulty conceiving, small litter sizes (1-2 kittens instead of typical 3-5), weak or nonviable kittens, poor milk production, or failure to enter estrus normally
  • Impaired platelet aggregation: Prolonged bleeding from minor injuries, easy bruising, or excessive bleeding during routine procedures like nail trimming
  • Growth problems in kittens: Stunted growth, failure to gain weight appropriately, poor muscle development, delayed weaning
  • Compromised immune function: Increased susceptibility to infections, slower recovery from illness, poor response to vaccination
  • Kidney dysfunction: Altered water balance, changes in urination frequency or volume (though kidney disease has many causes and requires veterinary diagnosis)

Severe, Long-Term Deficiency

Prolonged severe deficiency—most likely in cats fed inappropriate vegetarian/vegan diets or extremely low-fat diets—can cause life-threatening conditions:

  • Fatty liver disease (hepatic lipidosis): Though primarily associated with rapid weight loss or insufficient protein, severe fat deficiency can contribute to liver dysfunction
  • Complete reproductive failure: Total inability to reproduce, with reabsorption of fetuses or stillbirths
  • Severe immune compromise: Inability to fight off routine infections, leading to chronic illness
  • Death in severe cases: Particularly in kittens or cats with concurrent health problems

It's important to note that severe arachidonic acid deficiency is uncommon in cats eating commercial cat food, even lower-quality brands. Deficiency is primarily a risk for cats eating homemade diets improperly formulated, vegetarian/vegan diets, or dog food as their primary diet over extended periods.

Plant-Based Diets and Vegetarian/Vegan Feeding: Why They Cannot Work

The complete absence of arachidonic acid in plant sources makes vegetarian and vegan diets fundamentally incompatible with feline biology. This isn't a matter of careful formulation or supplementation—it's a basic mismatch between what cats require and what plants provide.

The Arachidonic Acid Problem

No plant contains arachidonic acid. Period. Flaxseed oil, corn oil, soybean oil, coconut oil, olive oil, sunflower oil, and every other plant fat contains zero arachidonic acid. These oils may contain abundant linoleic acid, but as we've established, cats cannot convert linoleic acid to arachidonic acid regardless of intake levels.

Some manufacturers of vegetarian cat foods claim that providing adequate linoleic acid addresses fatty acid needs. This is false. While linoleic acid is independently essential for cats (it supports basic cell membrane function and skin barrier integrity), it cannot replace arachidonic acid's specific functions in reproduction, platelet aggregation, immune response, and eicosanoid production.

The Supplementation Illusion

Some vegetarian cat foods attempt to address the arachidonic acid deficiency by adding synthetic arachidonic acid derived from fungal fermentation. While this theoretically could provide adequate amounts, several problems remain:

  • Other deficiencies remain unaddressed: Beyond arachidonic acid, plant-based diets also lack taurine, preformed vitamin A (retinol), vitamin B12, complete amino acid profiles, and appropriate protein levels—each requiring synthetic supplementation
  • Bioavailability concerns: Synthetic nutrients may not be absorbed or utilized as efficiently as nutrients from whole food sources
  • Long-term safety unknown: Cats have been studied eating meat-based diets for decades, but long-term controlled trials of heavily supplemented vegetarian diets are essentially nonexistent
  • Quality control issues: Synthetic nutrients require precise manufacturing and formulation; errors or degradation during storage can lead to deficiencies
  • Ethical concerns: Feeding an obligate carnivore a diet contrary to its evolutionary biology to satisfy human philosophical preferences constitutes a form of nutritional neglect

The Broader Nutritional Picture

Arachidonic acid deficiency is just one of many inevitable problems with plant-based cat diets. Cats also require:

  • Taurine: Found almost exclusively in animal tissues; plant sources contain negligible amounts
  • Preformed vitamin A (retinol): Cats cannot convert beta-carotene from plants to vitamin A and require retinol from animal sources
  • Vitamin B12 (cobalamin): Found only in animal products; no plant source provides bioavailable B12
  • High-quality complete protein: Cats require 30-40%+ protein on a dry matter basis with all essential amino acids in appropriate ratios—difficult to achieve with plant proteins
  • Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol): While some plants contain D2 (ergocalciferol), cats use D3 from animal sources much more efficiently
  • Appropriate mineral ratios: Plant-based diets often have inappropriate calcium:phosphorus ratios and phytates that inhibit mineral absorption

Creating a nutritionally adequate plant-based diet for cats would require such extensive synthetic supplementation that it essentially becomes a synthetic diet flavored with plant ingredients rather than a true vegetarian diet. At that point, the question becomes: why force a carnivore to eat plants when meat-based diets provide nutrition in the forms their bodies evolved to use?

Balancing Omega-6 and Omega-3 Fatty Acids

While ensuring adequate arachidonic acid intake is essential, the ratio of omega-6 to omega-3 fatty acids is equally important for optimal health. Arachidonic acid is an omega-6 fatty acid, and excessive omega-6 relative to omega-3 can promote chronic inflammation.

Understanding the Omega-6 to Omega-3 Balance

Omega-6 and omega-3 fatty acids compete for the same enzymes (COX and LOX) that produce eicosanoids. Omega-6 fatty acids—particularly arachidonic acid—generate predominantly pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (certain prostaglandins and leukotrienes that promote inflammation, pain, and immune activation). Omega-3 fatty acids—particularly EPA from fish—generate less inflammatory or anti-inflammatory eicosanoids (different prostaglandins, resolvins, and protectins that resolve inflammation and reduce pain).

The ratio of omega-6 to omega-3 in the diet determines which eicosanoids predominate. A diet with very high omega-6 and very low omega-3 (ratios of 30:1 or higher) creates a pro-inflammatory state, potentially worsening arthritis, kidney disease, allergies, and inflammatory bowel disease. A more balanced ratio (5:1 to 10:1) allows appropriate inflammatory responses when needed while preventing excessive chronic inflammation.

Optimal Ratios for Cats

Research suggests an omega-6 to omega-3 ratio between 5:1 and 10:1 is optimal for most cats. Individual needs vary based on health status:

  • Healthy adult cats: Ratios of 8:1 to 10:1 are acceptable and commonly found in quality commercial cat foods
  • Cats with inflammatory conditions: Ratios closer to 5:1 may be beneficial for cats with arthritis, chronic kidney disease, inflammatory bowel disease, or allergic skin conditions
  • Kittens and reproducing queens: Higher absolute amounts of both omega-6 and omega-3 are needed; ratios of 5:1 to 8:1 support growth and reproduction

Many commercial cat foods have omega-6 to omega-3 ratios of 10:1 to 20:1, which is higher than ideal but generally acceptable for healthy cats. The problem is not that these ratios are toxic, but that they may be suboptimal—particularly for cats with conditions that benefit from stronger anti-inflammatory support.

Practical Application: Supplementing Omega-3 While Maintaining Adequate Arachidonic Acid

The solution for cats with imbalanced fatty acid ratios is not to reduce arachidonic acid intake (which could cause deficiency), but to increase omega-3 intake to improve the ratio. This is where fish oil supplementation becomes valuable.

Example calculation for a 5 kg cat:

  • Current diet provides: 100 mg arachidonic acid daily (well above 50mg minimum for a 5kg cat at 0.02%)
  • Current diet provides: 50 mg EPA+DHA daily
  • Current ratio: 100:50 = 2:1 omega-6 (AA) to omega-3 (EPA+DHA)... wait, this seems wrong because AA is just one omega-6, and total omega-6 includes linoleic acid too.

Let me correct this. The omega-6 to omega-3 ratio includes all omega-6 fatty acids (linoleic acid, gamma-linolenic acid, arachidonic acid) compared to all omega-3 fatty acids (ALA, EPA, DHA). For cats, the relevant comparison is:

  • Total omega-6: Primarily linoleic acid (abundant) plus arachidonic acid (less abundant but biologically active)
  • Total omega-3: Primarily EPA and DHA from fish (ALA from plants is negligible for cats)

A cat eating a food with 2% total fat on an as-fed basis (about 8% dry matter) might be getting approximately:

  • 1,200 mg total omega-6 fatty acids daily (if omega-6 represents 60% of total fat)
  • 100 mg total omega-3 fatty acids daily (if omega-3 represents 5% of total fat)
  • Ratio: 1,200:100 = 12:1

To improve this ratio to 6:1, you'd need to double omega-3 intake to 200 mg daily. Adding fish oil providing 100 mg EPA+DHA daily achieves this while maintaining adequate arachidonic acid intake.

The critical principle: Never reduce arachidonic acid to improve ratios—cats need adequate absolute amounts. Always improve ratios by adding omega-3s from fish sources.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why can't cats make arachidonic acid like dogs can?

Cats lack sufficient delta-6-desaturase enzyme activity to efficiently convert linoleic acid (an omega-6 fatty acid found in plant oils) into arachidonic acid. While dogs possess robust enzymatic pathways to synthesize arachidonic acid from dietary linoleic acid, cats have virtually no conversion capability. As obligate carnivores, cats evolved eating prey rich in preformed arachidonic acid from animal tissues, so they never developed the biochemical machinery to produce it from plant-based precursors. This metabolic limitation makes arachidonic acid a dietary essential for cats but not for dogs.

How much arachidonic acid do cats need?

AAFCO standards require a minimum of 0.02% arachidonic acid on a dry matter basis for both adult maintenance and growth/reproduction in cats. For a typical cat eating 200-250 grams of dry food daily, this translates to approximately 40-50 mg of arachidonic acid per day. Most commercial cat foods exceed this minimum when formulated with animal fats like chicken fat or fish oil. Kittens, pregnant cats, and nursing queens may benefit from levels slightly above the minimum to support rapid growth and reproduction.

What are the best sources of arachidonic acid for cats?

The best sources of arachidonic acid are animal fats and tissues. Chicken fat is the richest practical source, containing approximately 800-1,200 mg per 100 grams. Other excellent sources include pork fat (400-600 mg per 100g), egg yolks (300-500 mg per 100g), organ meats like liver and kidney (150-400 mg per 100g), and fatty fish such as salmon or mackerel (200-300 mg per 100g). Plant sources contain zero arachidonic acid, making vegetarian or vegan diets completely inappropriate and dangerous for cats regardless of linoleic acid content.

What happens if a cat doesn't get enough arachidonic acid?

Arachidonic acid deficiency in cats causes multiple serious problems. Early signs include poor coat quality with dry, brittle fur and excessive shedding; scaly, flaky skin with delayed wound healing; and reproductive failure including poor conception rates, small litter sizes, and weak kittens. More severe deficiency leads to impaired platelet aggregation affecting blood clotting, compromised kidney function, abnormal inflammatory responses making cats more susceptible to infections, and fatty liver disease in extreme cases. Kittens experiencing deficiency during growth show stunted development and increased mortality. These symptoms typically develop over weeks to months of inadequate intake.

Can vegetarian or vegan diets provide enough arachidonic acid for cats?

No. Plant-based diets cannot provide arachidonic acid because it does not exist in plant sources—it is found exclusively in animal tissues. While plants contain linoleic acid (the precursor to arachidonic acid), cats cannot convert linoleic acid to arachidonic acid due to insufficient delta-6-desaturase enzyme activity. Feeding a cat a vegetarian or vegan diet will inevitably lead to arachidonic acid deficiency along with deficiencies in taurine, preformed vitamin A, and other essential nutrients. Vegetarian and vegan diets are fundamentally incompatible with feline obligate carnivore biology and constitute nutritional neglect.

Do commercial cat foods contain enough arachidonic acid?

Yes, the vast majority of commercial cat foods formulated to meet AAFCO standards contain adequate arachidonic acid. Foods using animal fats (chicken fat, fish oil, pork fat) and animal protein meals (chicken meal, fish meal) easily exceed the 0.02% minimum requirement. A typical dry cat food with 15-18% fat from animal sources provides 2-4 times the minimum arachidonic acid requirement. However, some lower-quality foods relying heavily on plant-based fats like soybean oil or canola oil may be marginal. Always check that the primary fat source is animal-derived when selecting commercial cat food.

How does arachidonic acid relate to inflammation in cats?

Arachidonic acid serves as the precursor to eicosanoids—signaling molecules including prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes that regulate inflammation, pain, fever, and immune responses. When tissues are injured or infected, enzymes release arachidonic acid from cell membranes, which is then converted into inflammatory mediators that recruit immune cells, increase blood flow, and initiate healing. While this inflammatory response is essential for fighting infections and healing wounds, excessive or chronic inflammation can be harmful. The balance between omega-6 fatty acids (like arachidonic acid) and omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) determines whether inflammation is appropriately regulated or becomes excessive. Cats need adequate arachidonic acid for healthy immune function, but also require omega-3s to balance inflammatory responses.

What is the ideal omega-6 to omega-3 ratio for cats?

Research suggests an omega-6 to omega-3 ratio between 5:1 and 10:1 is optimal for cats, though individual needs vary based on health status. Many commercial cat foods have ratios of 10:1 to 20:1, which is acceptable for healthy cats but may be too high for cats with inflammatory conditions like arthritis, kidney disease, or inflammatory bowel disease. Cats with these conditions benefit from ratios closer to 5:1 achieved through omega-3 supplementation with fish oil. The key is ensuring adequate arachidonic acid intake (meeting the 0.02% minimum) while also providing EPA and DHA from fish sources to balance inflammatory pathways. Simply reducing omega-6 intake without maintaining adequate arachidonic acid can cause deficiency.

Should I supplement my cat's diet with arachidonic acid?

Cats eating commercial cat foods formulated to AAFCO standards do not need arachidonic acid supplementation—they receive adequate amounts from animal fats in the food. Supplementation is only necessary for cats eating homemade diets that may be deficient, cats recovering from severe malnutrition, or cats with malabsorption disorders affecting fat digestion. If preparing homemade cat food, you must include animal fat sources like chicken fat, egg yolks, or fatty fish to ensure adequate arachidonic acid. Excessive arachidonic acid supplementation in cats already eating complete commercial foods provides no benefit and may promote excessive inflammation. Focus instead on balancing omega-6 and omega-3 ratios through fish oil supplementation if needed.

How is arachidonic acid different from linoleic acid?

Linoleic acid is an 18-carbon omega-6 fatty acid found abundantly in plant oils like corn oil, soybean oil, and sunflower oil. Arachidonic acid is a 20-carbon omega-6 fatty acid found exclusively in animal tissues. In most mammals, including dogs and humans, linoleic acid serves as a precursor that can be converted to arachidonic acid through enzymatic desaturation and elongation. However, cats lack sufficient delta-6-desaturase enzyme to perform this conversion efficiently, making linoleic acid and arachidonic acid both independently essential for cats. Linoleic acid supports skin barrier function and general membrane health, while arachidonic acid specifically enables reproduction, inflammatory responses, and platelet function. Cats require dietary sources of both fatty acids from their food.

Can cats get too much arachidonic acid?

Excessive arachidonic acid intake is uncommon in cats eating commercial foods, but theoretically high levels could promote excessive inflammation. Diets with extremely high animal fat content and very high omega-6 to omega-3 ratios (above 30:1) may contribute to chronic inflammatory conditions, though research specifically on arachidonic acid toxicity in cats is limited. More commonly, the problem is imbalance rather than absolute excess—adequate arachidonic acid paired with insufficient omega-3 fatty acids creates a pro-inflammatory state. For most cats, the practical concern is ensuring minimum adequate intake from animal sources while balancing with omega-3s from fish, not worrying about excessive arachidonic acid from typical cat foods.

Is arachidonic acid important for kitten development?

Yes, arachidonic acid is critical for kitten growth and development. It supports rapid cell membrane synthesis during growth, brain and nervous system development, immune system maturation, and reproductive organ development. Queen's milk naturally contains arachidonic acid, and kittens require adequate intake both from nursing and from weaning foods. AAFCO requirements specify the same 0.02% minimum for growth/reproduction as for adult maintenance, though some researchers suggest kittens may benefit from slightly higher levels. Deficiency during kittenhood causes stunted growth, poor coat development, increased susceptibility to infections, and higher mortality rates. All commercial kitten foods formulated to AAFCO growth standards contain adequate arachidonic acid from animal fat sources.
