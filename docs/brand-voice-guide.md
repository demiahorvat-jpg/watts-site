# Watts Brand Voice Guide

**Last Updated:** March 9, 2026

---

## The Big Idea

**Better Each Day.**

Watts exists because the best health habits—for humans and dogs—are built long term and compound over time. We're not selling a quick fix. We're a daily ritual that adds up.

## Brand Personality

Watts is **Optimistic, Thoughtful, Spirited, and Expert.**

| Trait | What it means in practice |
|-------|---------------------------|
| **Optimistic** | We believe in what's possible. We talk about what dogs *can* be, not what's wrong with them. Forward-looking, never fear-based. |
| **Thoughtful** | We've done the work. We cite research when it matters, but we don't lecture. We respect the reader's intelligence. |
| **Spirited** | We have energy. Our voice has movement—like a dog mid-zoomie. We're allowed to be playful, even a little cheeky. |
| **Expert** | We know our stuff. When we make a claim, it's backed by something real. No hedging, no "may help support." |

## Voice Principles

### 1. Alive, not polished

Write like the photography looks: motion blur, a little wild, full of life. Sentences can be short. Punchy. They don't all need to be grammatically perfect if the rhythm is right.

**Yes:** "Today's treats. Tomorrow's health."
**No:** "Our treats provide daily nutrition that supports long-term wellness outcomes."

### 2. Clever, not cute

Wordplay is encouraged—it's a Watts signature. But it should feel smart, not forced.

**Yes:** "Organ-ized." / "Livelier with Liver" / "Meet the meat (stick)."
**No:** "Paws-itively delicious!" / "Fur real, though!"

The difference: Watts puns are dry, almost deadpan. They don't wink too hard.

### 3. Confident, not preachy

We know what we're talking about. We don't need to oversell or hedge. State things directly.

**Yes:** "Beef muscle and organ meat, no filler."
**No:** "We believe in using only the highest quality ingredients that may help support your dog's overall wellness journey."

### 4. Real, not clinical

We talk like a knowledgeable friend, not a veterinary textbook. Use imagery people can feel.

**Yes:** "Works like medicine but tastes like scraps from the table."
**No:** "Formulated to deliver optimal bioavailable nutrients for enhanced metabolic function."

### 5. Daily ritual, not miracle cure

Everything ties back to consistency over time. We're playing the long game.

**Yes:** "Keeping them coming back for more each and every day."
**No:** "Transform your dog's health instantly!"

## Headline Style

Headlines are bold, often short, sometimes a single punchy phrase.

**Examples:**
- Ridiculously Nutritious.
- That good.
- For Future Wags.
- Better Dailies.
- Life Expansion.
- What's a watts?

**Characteristics:**
- Title case or sentence case (not ALL CAPS)
- Often end with a period for weight, even when short
- Can be playful questions or statements
- Avoid generic benefit claims ("The Best Dog Treats")

---

## Body Copy Style

Body copy is lowercase, conversational, and grounded. It flows naturally when read aloud.

**Example:**
> made from the best stuff on earth, our treat works like medicine but tastes like scraps from the table, keeping them coming back for more each and every day.

**Characteristics:**
- Lowercase openings are acceptable (especially in shorter marketing copy)
- Sentences connect with natural rhythm
- Uses concrete imagery ("scraps from the table") over abstract benefits
- Doesn't over-explain—trusts the reader

## Blog & Long-Form Content

For educational content (blog posts, ingredient deep-dives), the voice adapts slightly:

### Keep:
- Confidence and directness
- Real-world language over jargon
- Specific numbers and evidence (this is where "Expert" shines)
- Personality in headlines and intros

### Adjust:
- More structure (clear sections, FAQs, takeaways)
- Standard capitalization and punctuation
- Citations for claims
- Slightly more formal than marketing copy, but never stiff

### Tone target:
**"A knowledgeable friend who's done the research."**

Not an academic paper. Not a sales pitch. Somewhere in between—credible but approachable.

**Good blog voice:**
> Most dogs don't need digestive enzyme supplements—their pancreas produces enough on its own. But for dogs with EPI, chronic pancreatitis, or age-related decline, enzymes can be genuinely helpful. Here's what the research actually says.

**Too clinical:**
> Exogenous digestive enzyme supplementation has been evaluated in canine subjects with varying degrees of pancreatic insufficiency...

**Too casual/salesy:**
> OMG you guys, digestive enzymes are AMAZING for dogs! Your pup is going to feel SO much better!

## Words We Use

| Instead of... | We say... |
|---------------|-----------|
| wellness journey | health / feeling good |
| fur baby / pupper | dog |
| pet parent | owner (or just "you") |
| holistic | whole-body / real ingredients |
| premium | quality / the good stuff |
| scientifically formulated | backed by research |
| may help support | helps / supports (be direct) |
| utilize | use |
| purchase | buy / get |

---

## Words We Avoid

- **Fear-based language:** "toxins," "dangerous," "you could be harming your dog"
- **Over-the-top enthusiasm:** "AMAZING," "incredible," "game-changer"
- **Cutesy pet speak:** "fur baby," "hooman," "pupper," "doggo," "bork"
- **Vague hedging:** "may help," "could potentially," "some studies suggest"
- **Corporate jargon:** "leverage," "synergy," "optimize"
- **Competitor bashing:** Never name or attack other brands

## The Watts Feeling

The brand deck describes it perfectly:

> "Letting loose. We're seeing dogs being dogs, motion blur, out of focus, crazy eyes and all. The watts feeling."

This applies to voice too. Watts copy should feel:
- **Energetic** but not manic
- **Unpolished** but not sloppy
- **Joyful** but not saccharine
- **Smart** but not showing off

It's the feeling of a dog sprinting toward a sprinkler. Full commitment, pure enjoyment, no self-consciousness.
