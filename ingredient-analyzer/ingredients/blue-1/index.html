What Is Blue 1?

Blue 1 (Brilliant Blue FCF) is a synthetic petroleum-derived dye used to create blue and green colors in pet food. Part of the FDA-certified synthetic color family alongside red-40, yellow-5, yellow-6, blue-2, and red-3—all petroleum-based dyes that provide zero nutritional value but face varying levels of regulatory scrutiny and consumer concern.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. beet juice color: Blue 1 is a synthetic petroleum-derived dye (FD&C Blue No. 1), while beet juice color is a natural plant-based colorant. Natural colorants are preferred in premium pet foods.
  • vs. turmeric: Blue 1 is synthetic with no nutritional value and purely cosmetic, while turmeric provides natural yellow/orange color plus anti-inflammatory benefits. Turmeric adds functional value.

Why Manufacturers Add Blue 1 to Dog Food

Blue 1 (Brilliant Blue FCF) is a synthetic petroleum-derived dye with zero nutritional value — it creates blue and green colors that appeal to human buyers, since dogs have dichromatic vision and are largely indifferent to food color, making this ingredient purely a marketing tool at the pet's expense.

  • Creates visually appealing blue or green color (when blended with yellow-5 or yellow-6)
  • Makes food look more appealing to humans
  • Provides color consistency across batches
  • Masks ingredient variations
  • No nutritional or functional benefit for dogs

Like all synthetic colors including red-40 and the yellow dyes, blue-1 exists purely for human visual psychology despite all being petroleum-derived and FDA-certified additives.

Blue 1 Quality Considerations

Blue 1 presence signals a manufacturer prioritizing visual marketing over nutrition. There's no quality spectrum here—the ingredient is simply unnecessary. Premium brands universally avoid synthetic dyes. If you see Blue 1 alongside other artificial colors (Red 40, Yellow 5/6), the product is formulated for shelf appeal, not pet health.

Blue 1: Potential Concerns

While Blue 1 is FDA-approved, pet owners should be aware that: (1) synthetic dyes provide zero nutritional value and serve only cosmetic purposes for humans, not dogs, (2) Blue 1 has been linked to hypersensitivity reactions and allergic responses in some dogs, (3) concerns about potential carcinogenic contaminants exist, though the FDA considers approved levels safe, and (4) many countries require warning labels on foods containing synthetic dyes due to health concerns. Individual dogs may respond differently to the same ingredient based on their health status, age, and sensitivities.

Blue 1: What the Research Shows

Blue 1 (Brilliant Blue FCF) is a synthetic blue dye derived from petroleum, used in pet food to create blue and green hues. While generally considered less controversial than some other artificial colors, it has still been linked to allergic reactions and is avoided by many natural pet food brands.

Key Research Findings

  • Blue 1 was not included in the Southampton study that found hyperactivity links with certain other artificial colors, suggesting it may have less behavioral impact (McCann et al., The Lancet, 2007)
  • The FDA permits Blue 1 in food and feed, considering it safe at approved levels based on toxicology studies (FDA Color Additive Status List)
  • In 2003, the FDA investigated reports of Blue 1 causing deaths in dialysis patients, though the issue was contamination of the dye batch rather than Blue 1 itself (FDA safety alerts, 2003)
  • Some research has suggested that Blue 1 can cross the blood-brain barrier when combined with other agents, raising theoretical concerns about neurological effects, though clinical significance remains unclear (Neurotoxicology research)
  • Blue 1 has been associated with allergic reactions in sensitive individuals, including hives and itching, though less frequently than Yellow 5 (Allergy case reports)
  • The European Union permits Blue 1 (E133) without the warning labels required for colors like Red 40 and Yellow 5 (EU food regulations)

Evidence Level: FDA-approved with a generally better safety profile than some other artificial colors. Less evidence of hyperactivity compared to Red 40 and Yellow 5. Concerns about blood-brain barrier penetration require more research. Allergic reactions possible but uncommon. Still avoided by natural brands on principle (no nutritional value, petroleum-derived).

How Blue 1 Is Made & Used

Blue 1, officially known as Brilliant Blue FCF, is a triphenylmethane dye synthesized from petroleum-derived aromatic compounds through a multi-stage chemical process. Unlike the azo dyes like Red 40 and Yellow 5, Blue 1 has a different chemical structure that gives it that distinctive blue color. Production involves condensation reactions between specific aromatic compounds, followed by sulfonation to make the dye water-soluble. Like all certified food colors, each batch must pass FDA purity testing before it can legally go into human or pet food products, checking for heavy metals, unreacted materials, and other potential contaminants.

Practical Applications in Pet Products

Blue 1 appears less frequently in dog food than Red 40 or yellow dyes, but it has specific uses that keep it in the market. Manufacturers include it at very low levels, typically 0.001% to 0.006%, either to create genuinely blue-colored treats or more commonly to blend with yellow dyes for green hues. That green color is supposed to suggest vegetables or "garden" ingredients, even in products where actual vegetable content is minimal. The dye is extremely heat-stable, surviving the high temperatures of kibble extrusion and baking processes without breaking down. It's also photostable, meaning it won't fade significantly under store lighting or during the product's shelf life. For manufacturers creating visually distinctive products, particularly treats aimed at human purchasers who want something "fun" for their dogs, Blue 1 offers consistent, cheap coloring.

Economic Considerations and Market Position

Blue 1 costs about $28 to $40 per kilogram for certified food-grade material, making it one of the pricier synthetic dyes. However, with inclusion rates measured in parts per million, the actual cost impact on finished pet food is negligible, usually pennies per ton. The FDA approves Blue 1 for use in both human and pet food, setting maximum levels at 150 parts per million in pet food products. AAFCO also permits its use. Interestingly, Blue 1 didn't make it into the EU's list of dyes requiring warning labels, unlike Red 40, Yellow 5, and Yellow 6, which suggests regulatory bodies consider it slightly less problematic from a behavioral standpoint. That said, premium pet food brands still avoid it on principle. The ingredient has become associated with budget and mid-tier products where visual novelty matters more than ingredient story. You'll find it mainly in colorful treats and specialty products where manufacturers want to create visual impact on crowded retail shelves.

Finding Blue 1 on Pet Food Labels

Blue 1 (Brilliant Blue FCF) provides no nutritional value — it's added to color food for human visual appeal, as dogs don't perceive colors the same way we do. Its presence signals a formulation that prioritized appearance marketing over clean-label simplicity.:

What to Look For

  • Look for 'Blue 1,' 'Blue #1,' or 'Brilliant Blue' in ingredient lists
  • Common in blue-colored treats and kibble, or combined with Yellow 5 to create green
  • Less common than Red 40, Yellow 5, or Yellow 6 in pet food
  • Premium brands advertise "No artificial colors"

Alternative Names

This ingredient may also appear as:

  • Blue 1 (U.S. name)
  • Blue #1 (alternative notation)
  • Brilliant Blue FCF (chemical name)
  • E133 (European food additive code)
  • FD&C Blue No. 1 (FDA designation)

Red Flags

  • Blue 1 in products marketed as "natural" or "holistic"
  • Multiple artificial colors creating bright blue or green kibble
  • Blue-colored treats with no functional purpose

Green Flags

  • "No artificial colors" or "Naturally colored"
  • Natural blue/purple from blueberries, spirulina, or purple carrot
  • Uncolored products where natural ingredient color shows through

Typical Position: Blue 1 appears near the end of ingredient lists due to minimal amounts used. Even trace quantities color entire batches blue or green. Like all artificial colors, the issue isn't quantity but necessity—dogs don't care about food color.

Watts' Take

Blue 1 is completely unnecessary and potentially harmful. It's added solely to make food look appealing to humans—dogs don't care if their food is blue. Like all synthetic dyes, it comes with health concerns including allergies and contamination risks. Quality brands avoid all synthetic colorants. If a food contains Blue 1 or any FD&C dyes, it signals the manufacturer prioritizes marketing over health. Choose foods with no artificial colors or, if color is used, natural sources like spirulina or turmeric.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Blue 1 used in dog food if dogs can't see color well?

Blue 1 is added purely for human appeal—it makes kibble look more 'meaty' or visually interesting to pet owners. Dogs can see some colors but don't choose food based on appearance. This dye provides zero nutritional value and exists only for marketing purposes, which is why we recommend avoiding it.

Is Blue 1 (Brilliant Blue FCF) safe for dogs?

While approved for use, Blue 1 has been linked to potential health concerns in studies, including possible behavioral effects and hypersensitivity reactions. The European Food Safety Authority has raised concerns. For an ingredient that provides no nutritional benefit whatsoever, even small risks aren't worth taking.

What are better alternatives to artificial dyes like Blue 1?

Quality dog foods simply don't use artificial dyes—the food's natural color is fine since dogs don't care. If a brand wants visual appeal, natural colorants like spirulina, beet juice, or turmeric can be used safely. But the best approach is no added colorants at all, which is what premium brands typically do.
