What It Is

Byproduct of cheese production, containing milk proteins (primarily whey protein), lactose, vitamins, and minerals in dried form.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. colostrum: Dried whey is a cheese byproduct used as a protein source and binder, while colostrum is first milk with immune-supporting antibodies. Colostrum is more functional.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include dried whey in dog food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Source of easily digestible milk protein
  • Rich in branched-chain amino acids
  • Adds palatability and flavor
  • Provides calcium and B vitamins
  • Binding agent in treats and kibble

Quality Considerations

When evaluating dried whey in dog products, it's important to understand protein density, amino acid profile, digestibility, and sourcing quality. This ingredient's quality and appropriateness can vary significantly based on sourcing, processing, and the specific formula it's used in.

Quality Note

Quality protein source with good amino acid profile, but many dogs are lactose intolerant and may experience digestive upset. Contains less protein per weight than whey protein concentrate or isolate. Primarily used for flavor and binding rather than as primary protein source.

Potential Concerns

While dried whey provides protein and lactose, pet owners should be aware that: (1) many adult dogs have reduced lactase enzyme and cannot properly digest lactose, leading to diarrhea and gas, (2) dairy is a common allergen in dogs, particularly the casein and whey proteins, (3) whey is a byproduct of cheese-making and quality can vary significantly, and (4) the high lactose content makes it unsuitable for lactose-intolerant dogs. Individual dogs may respond differently to the same ingredient based on their health status, age, and sensitivities.

Scientific Evidence

Dried whey is a dairy by-product created by removing moisture from liquid whey, the watery portion of milk that separates during cheese production. It contains lactose, proteins, minerals, and trace amounts of fat. In dog food, dried whey serves multiple purposes: palatability enhancement, protein supplementation, and as a source of lactose and minerals.

Key Research Findings

  • Dried whey is approximately 70-75% lactose, 11-14% protein, and contains minerals including calcium and phosphorus
  • The lactose content provides a slightly sweet flavor that dogs generally find palatable, improving food acceptance
  • Whey protein contains all essential amino acids and has high biological value, though the protein content in dried whey is relatively low compared to dedicated protein sources
  • Most adult dogs have reduced lactase enzyme production and may experience digestive upset (gas, diarrhea, bloating) from significant lactose intake
  • At low inclusion rates (1-3%), most dogs tolerate dried whey without issues; higher amounts increase the risk of lactose intolerance symptoms
  • Dried whey provides some calcium and phosphorus, though it's not typically a primary mineral source in formulas
  • AAFCO recognizes dried whey as safe for use in pet food

Evidence Level: Well-established for palatability enhancement. Moderate evidence for digestive tolerance issues in lactose-sensitive dogs at higher inclusion levels.

How to Spot on Labels

What to Look For

Dried whey appears most frequently in puppy formulas, training treats, and foods designed for palatability. Its position reveals whether it's a minor palatant or a more significant ingredient. Dogs with known dairy sensitivities should avoid foods containing dried whey or other dairy products.

Alternative Names

  • Dried whey — The standard listing
  • Whey — Shortened version
  • Whey powder — Indicates powdered form
  • Sweet whey — Specifies the type (from cheese production)

Red Flags

  • High position (before 10th ingredient) — Suggests significant lactose content, which may cause digestive upset in lactose-sensitive dogs
  • In formulas for adult dogs with sensitivities — Adult dogs produce less lactase and may not tolerate dairy well
  • Multiple dairy ingredients — Dried whey + whey protein + milk protein may indicate excessive dairy reliance

Green Flags

  • Low position (after 15th ingredient) — Small amounts for palatability are generally well-tolerated
  • In puppy formulas — Puppies have more lactase enzyme and tolerate dairy better than adult dogs
  • In training treats — The high palatability makes dried whey useful in small-portion treats

Typical Position: Dried whey typically appears in positions 10-20 in puppy formulas and training treats. It's less common in adult maintenance formulas, where it appears lower (20-30) if included at all.

Watts' Take

Decent supplementary protein with good palatability, but watch for lactose intolerance in sensitive dogs. Best in small amounts. If your dog tolerates dairy, it's a beneficial ingredient. Not suitable as a primary protein source.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is dried whey safe for dogs?

Generally yes, but dried whey is concentrated dairy and may cause issues in lactose-intolerant dogs. Dried whey contains lactose and can trigger digestive upset, gas, or diarrhea in sensitive dogs. It's used for palatability and protein supplementation. If your dog has dairy sensitivities or lactose intolerance, avoid foods containing dried whey.

What does dried whey do in dog food?

Byproduct of cheese production, containing milk proteins (primarily whey protein), lactose, vitamins, and minerals in dried form. Dog food manufacturers include this ingredient to provide source of easily digestible milk protein and provide rich in branched-chain amino acids.

Can dried whey cause allergies in dogs?

Any protein source can potentially trigger allergies in sensitive dogs. Signs of food allergies include itchy skin, ear infections, digestive issues, and excessive paw licking. If your dog shows these symptoms, consult your veterinarian about an elimination diet to identify the trigger ingredient.

How much dried whey should be in dog food?

Quality dog foods typically list primary protein sources like dried whey within the first 3-5 ingredients. The exact percentage varies by formula, but protein-focused foods generally contain 25-35% total protein for adults, and 28-40% for puppies or performance dogs. Check the guaranteed analysis rather than relying solely on ingredient order.
