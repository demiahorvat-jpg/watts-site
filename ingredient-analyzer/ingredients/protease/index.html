What It Is

A digestive enzyme that breaks down proteins into amino acids and smaller peptides for absorption. Commonly added to digestive supplements.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. bromelain: Both are protein-digesting enzymes. Protease is a general term for any protein-breaking enzyme (often microbial), while bromelain is a specific protease from pineapple with anti-inflammatory properties.
  • vs. papain: Both are proteolytic enzymes that break down proteins. Protease is a broad category (often from bacteria/fungi), while papain is a specific plant protease from papaya.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include protease in dog food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Helps break down dietary proteins
  • May reduce protein-related digestive upset
  • Supports nutrient absorption
  • Can benefit dogs with exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI)

Quality Considerations

When evaluating protease in dog products, it's important to understand clinical evidence, appropriate dosing, and targeted health benefits. This ingredient's quality and appropriateness can vary significantly based on sourcing, processing, and the specific formula it's used in.

Quality Note

Dogs naturally produce proteases (trypsin, pepsin), but supplementation may help dogs with digestive disorders or those transitioning to higher-protein diets. Quality depends on enzyme activity units (HUT - Hemoglobin Unit Tyrosine).

Scientific Evidence

Protease is a broad category of enzymes that break down proteins into smaller peptides and amino acids. In pet supplements, proteases are added to support protein digestion, though healthy dogs produce adequate digestive enzymes naturally.

Key Research Findings

  • Proteases cleave peptide bonds in dietary proteins, breaking them into absorbable amino acids and small peptides
  • Dogs naturally produce proteases (pepsin in the stomach, trypsin and chymotrypsin from the pancreas) for protein digestion
  • Supplemental proteases are most beneficial for dogs with exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI) or other digestive disorders, not healthy dogs
  • Different proteases work at different pH levels; comprehensive formulas include multiple types (acid, neutral, and alkaline proteases)
  • Protease activity is measured in various units (HUT - Hemoglobin Unit Tyrosine; USP units), and standardization ensures consistent potency
  • Limited evidence that protease supplementation benefits healthy dogs with normal pancreatic function
  • Generally safe; enzymes are proteins that are themselves digested if not active

Evidence Level: Strong evidence for benefit in dogs with EPI or maldigestion. Weak evidence for benefits in healthy dogs with normal digestive function.

How to Spot on Labels

What to Look For

Protease appears in digestive enzyme supplements, some specialty foods, and products marketed for digestive support. Since the term "protease" is broad (covering many enzyme types), quality products specify enzyme types and activity units. Context and standardization matter significantly.

Alternative Names

  • Protease — General category name
  • Acid protease — Works in stomach (low pH)
  • Alkaline protease — Works in intestines (high pH)
  • Fungal protease — Source specification
  • Bacterial protease — Source specification

Green Flags

  • Activity units specified (e.g., "protease [50,000 HUT]") — Standardized, quality-controlled enzyme
  • Multiple protease types listed — Comprehensive pH range coverage
  • In digestive support supplements — Appropriate use for targeted digestive concerns
  • Combined with other digestive enzymes — Amylase, lipase create full-spectrum support

Red Flags

  • No activity units specified — Unknown potency; could be ineffective
  • In heavily processed kibble — High heat likely deactivates enzymes

What's Normal

Protease supplementation is most beneficial for dogs with diagnosed digestive disorders. For healthy dogs, benefit is unclear. Look for standardized activity units and appropriate product formats (supplements, freeze-dried) for enzyme stability.

Typical Position: In enzyme supplements, protease appears in positions 3-10. Meaningful inclusion requires specified activity levels.

Watts' Take

Valuable digestive enzyme for dogs with protein digestion issues or pancreatic conditions. Most beneficial when combined with other digestive enzymes in a full-spectrum formula.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does protease do in dog food?

Protease breaks down proteins into smaller peptides and amino acids, making them easier to absorb. This is particularly helpful for dogs with compromised digestion, seniors with declining enzyme production, or dogs recovering from GI issues. It may also help reduce protein-related gas and bloating by ensuring more complete protein digestion before bacteria can ferment undigested protein.

Does protease survive kibble processing?

Heat destroys most enzymes, so protease in kibble must be added after cooking or be heat-stable varieties. Fungal-derived proteases (from Aspergillus) tend to be more heat-resistant than animal-derived versions. Quality manufacturers add enzymes as a coating after extrusion or use protected forms. If protease appears in the main ingredient panel, it's likely destroyed during processing.

Which dogs benefit most from added protease?

Dogs with exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI) need enzyme supplementation, including protease. Seniors with declining digestive function, dogs with chronic digestive issues, and dogs eating high-protein diets may also benefit. However, healthy dogs with normal pancreatic function produce adequate protease naturally. Added protease won't hurt, but provides limited benefit for dogs with healthy digestion.

Related Ingredients

Bromelain

A specific plant-derived protease from pineapple with anti-inflammatory properties. Often included alongside microbial proteases for comprehensive protein digestion support.

Papain

Plant-based protease from papaya that works across a wide pH range. Frequently combined with other proteases in digestive supplements for synergistic protein breakdown.

Lipase

Fat-digesting enzyme commonly paired with protease in full-spectrum digestive supplements. Together they support breakdown of proteins and fats for complete nutrient absorption.

Amylase

Starch-digesting enzyme that works alongside protease in comprehensive enzyme formulas. Together they cover protein and carbohydrate digestion.

Cellulase

Fiber-digesting enzyme often included with protease in enzyme blends. Both support complete digestion of protein-rich foods that also contain plant ingredients.
