Quick Summary

Thiamine Mononitrate is vitamin B1 in its most heat-stable form—critical since extrusion destroys B vitamins. Deficiency causes serious neurological problems including seizures. Raw fish diets are especially risky due to thiaminase enzymes. Water-soluble, so excess is safely excreted. Standard in complete diets; its presence is normal.

What It Is

Supplemental B1 for energy metabolism and nervous system function. Thiamine (vitamin B1) is available in two common supplemental forms: thiamine mononitrate (the nitrate salt, more stable) and thiamine hydrochloride (the hydrochloride salt, slightly more bioavailable). Both are synthetic forms that effectively provide vitamin B1. Thiamine works together with riboflavin, niacin, and other B vitamins in the B complex to support metabolic pathways. Since processing can destroy natural B vitamins in food, they're typically all supplemented to ensure complete nutrition. Essential for carbohydrate metabolism and proper muscle function including the heart.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include thiamine mononitrate in pet food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Energy metabolism
  • Nervous system health
  • Essential B vitamin

Part of the B vitamin complex including riboflavin, niacin, pantothenic acid, pyridoxine, biotin, and folic acid—all working synergistically in energy metabolism and cellular function.

Scientific Evidence

Thiamine mononitrate is a stable, supplemental form of vitamin B1 (thiamine), an essential vitamin critical for energy metabolism, nervous system function, and overall health. Dogs cannot synthesize thiamine and must obtain it from their diet.

Key Research Findings

  • Thiamine is essential for carbohydrate metabolism, serving as a coenzyme in energy production pathways
  • Critical for proper nervous system function, muscle function, and heart health
  • AAFCO requirement for adult maintenance: 1.0mg per 1000 kcal; for growth/reproduction: 1.0mg per 1000 kcal
  • Thiamine deficiency causes serious neurological problems including seizures, loss of appetite, and heart failure; though rare in complete diets, it can occur with improper storage or certain raw fish
  • Thiamine mononitrate is more heat-stable than thiamine hydrochloride, making it suitable for kibble manufacturing
  • Excess thiamine is excreted (water-soluble vitamin); toxicity is not a concern at supplemental levels
  • Bioavailable and effective form for meeting nutritional requirements

Evidence Level: Strong evidence for thiamine essentiality and thiamine mononitrate as an effective supplemental form. Critical for nervous system and energy metabolism. Required in complete diets.

Forms & Stability

Two forms exist: thiamine mononitrate (more heat-stable) and thiamine hydrochloride (slightly more bioavailable). Pet food uses mononitrate because thiamine is exceptionally heat-sensitive—it degrades at temperatures above 100°C, and kibble extrusion reaches 120-180°C. Synthetic thiamine is identical to natural once absorbed.

Processing Overage

Manufacturers add 20-50% extra thiamine beyond target levels to compensate for heat degradation during processing and storage. This overage is safe—thiamine is water-soluble and excess is excreted in urine. Some premium manufacturers apply thiamine post-extrusion as a topical coating to minimize heat exposure.

Deficiency Risks

Thiamine deficiency causes serious neurological problems including seizures, loss of coordination, and heart failure. Rare in commercial pet foods due to routine supplementation, but can occur with poorly formulated homemade diets or raw fish diets containing thiaminase enzymes that destroy thiamine.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is thiamine mononitrate used instead of thiamine hydrochloride?

Thiamine mononitrate is more heat-stable than thiamine hydrochloride, making it better suited for kibble manufacturing where extrusion temperatures reach 120-180°C (250-350°F). Thiamine is exceptionally heat-sensitive among B vitamins, degrading above 100°C. While hydrochloride is slightly more bioavailable, mononitrate better survives processing. Manufacturers typically add 20-50% extra to account for heat losses.

What happens if a dog doesn't get enough thiamine?

Thiamine deficiency causes serious neurological problems including seizures, loss of coordination, appetite loss, and heart failure. Though rare in complete commercial diets, deficiency can occur with poorly formulated homemade diets or raw fish diets—certain raw fish contain thiaminase enzymes that destroy thiamine. This is why commercial foods always supplement with stable forms like thiamine mononitrate.

Can dogs get too much thiamine?

No. Thiamine is water-soluble, so excess is excreted in urine rather than accumulating in the body. There's no documented toxicity at supplemental levels in dog food. Manufacturers intentionally include 20-50% more than target levels to compensate for heat degradation during processing and storage—this overage is safe and ensures adequate thiamine throughout the product's shelf life.
