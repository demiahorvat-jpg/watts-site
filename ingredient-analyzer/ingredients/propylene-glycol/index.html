What It Is

Propylene glycol is a synthetic liquid alcohol used as a humectant—a moisture-retaining agent—in semi-moist dog food and treats. It's a clear, viscous, slightly sweet-tasting substance that keeps products soft, chewy, and pliable by preventing moisture loss. Chemically known as 1,2-propanediol, it's the same compound used in some food products, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals, though pet food applications are distinct from other uses like antifreeze (which uses the toxic cousin, ethylene glycol).

While propylene glycol is FDA-approved for use in dog food in the United States, it's completely banned in cat food due to documented toxicity in felines. This raises reasonable concerns for dog owners, even though dogs metabolize propylene glycol differently than cats. The ingredient provides no nutritional value—its sole purpose is to maintain the soft, moist texture of semi-moist foods and prevent mold growth by reducing water activity in the product.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. glycerin: Glycerin (glycerol) is a natural humectant derived from plant oils or animal fats. It performs the same function as propylene glycol but is considered safer and more natural. Premium brands choose glycerin over propylene glycol.
  • vs. honey: Honey is a natural humectant that can help retain moisture in treats while providing trace nutrients. It's more expensive and less effective than propylene glycol but completely natural.
  • vs. sorbitol: Sorbitol is a sugar alcohol used as a humectant in some pet foods. It's generally recognized as safe but can cause digestive upset in high amounts.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include propylene glycol in semi-moist dog products specifically to achieve and maintain the soft, chewy texture that consumers expect from these products. It serves several functional purposes:

  • Retains moisture to keep products soft and chewy
  • Prevents mold and bacterial growth by reducing water activity
  • Extends shelf life of semi-moist products
  • Creates consistent texture across production batches
  • Less expensive than natural alternatives like vegetable glycerin
  • Effective at low inclusion rates (typically 5-15% of product)

Quality Considerations

When evaluating propylene glycol in dog products, the most important consideration is that it's completely unnecessary from a nutritional standpoint. Its presence indicates a semi-moist format, which is inherently more processed than dry kibble or wet canned food. Premium brands that produce semi-moist treats typically avoid propylene glycol in favor of natural alternatives like vegetable glycerin or simply choose freeze-dried or dehydrated formats that don't require humectants at all.

The concentration matters significantly. Lower positioning on the ingredient list is preferable, though propylene glycol often appears in the first 5-10 ingredients due to its relatively high inclusion rate (5-15%). Products marketed as "natural" or "holistic" should not contain propylene glycol, as it directly contradicts these claims. The safest approach is to avoid propylene glycol entirely, especially for dogs with sensitivities, digestive issues, or compromised health.

Potential Concerns

While propylene glycol is approved for dog food, it's banned in cat food due to causing Heinz body formation and red blood cell damage in felines. Some dogs may experience digestive upset, increased thirst, or mild allergic reactions. Long-term effects of regular propylene glycol consumption in dogs haven't been extensively studied. Dogs with kidney or liver issues should avoid it due to metabolic demands. The fact that it's unnecessary and has safer alternatives (vegetable glycerin) makes it an avoidable risk. Premium brands exclude it, suggesting industry leaders view it as suboptimal.

Scientific Evidence

Propylene glycol works as a humectant by attracting and binding water molecules, preventing moisture from evaporating from semi-moist foods. This keeps the product soft and pliable while simultaneously reducing the "free water" available for microbial growth, thereby acting as a preservative. The compound is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) for dogs by the FDA, with established safe inclusion levels typically not exceeding 15% of product weight.

However, the safety profile differs dramatically between species. In cats, propylene glycol consumption leads to Heinz body anemia—a condition where red blood cells are damaged due to oxidative stress. This led to an outright ban on propylene glycol in cat food in the 1990s. While dogs metabolize propylene glycol more efficiently than cats and don't develop the same hematological issues, some dogs do experience gastrointestinal upset, particularly with higher consumption levels or regular long-term exposure.

The lack of extensive long-term studies on chronic propylene glycol consumption in dogs is notable. Most safety data comes from acute toxicity studies rather than lifetime feeding trials. Given that propylene glycol provides zero nutritional benefit and safer alternatives exist (vegetable glycerin, natural humectants), many veterinarians and pet nutrition experts recommend avoiding it when possible, particularly for dogs with pre-existing health conditions.

Key Research Findings

  • Propylene glycol is FDA-approved for dog food at levels up to 15% but completely banned in cat food due to causing Heinz body anemia in felines
  • Dogs metabolize propylene glycol through hepatic alcohol dehydrogenase, converting it to lactic acid, which is then processed through normal metabolic pathways
  • Some dogs experience digestive upset, increased thirst, or mild osmotic diarrhea when consuming products with higher propylene glycol levels (>10%)
  • Long-term feeding studies on propylene glycol in dogs are limited; most safety data comes from short-term acute toxicity studies rather than lifetime exposure research
  • Propylene glycol provides no nutritional value—its sole function is moisture retention and mold prevention in semi-moist formats
  • Natural alternatives like vegetable glycerin perform the same function without the safety concerns, explaining why premium brands avoid propylene glycol
  • The ban on propylene glycol in cat food and its continued use in dog food creates concern among pet owners seeking to minimize synthetic additives across all pets in multi-pet households

Evidence Level: Moderate evidence for safety in dogs at approved levels, but significant concerns based on feline toxicity and lack of long-term canine studies. Strong evidence for functional effectiveness as a humectant, but equally strong evidence that natural alternatives (vegetable glycerin) can perform the same function more safely.

Manufacturing & Real-World Usage

Synthetic Production Process

Propylene glycol manufacturing starts with propylene oxide, which reacts with water under heat and pressure to produce 1,2-propanediol. The process is entirely synthetic, using petrochemical feedstocks as the starting material. What you end up with is a clear, slightly viscous liquid that's sweet-tasting and highly hygroscopic, meaning it aggressively pulls moisture from the environment. This moisture-attracting property makes it effective as a humectant, but it also means the compound comes from industrial chemical production rather than natural sources.

The food-grade propylene glycol used in pet products meets purity specifications that exclude toxic contaminants, but it's still fundamentally a synthetic chemical. This bothers some consumers more than others. For manufacturers, the appeal has historically been cost and effectiveness - propylene glycol worked well and cost less than natural alternatives. However, as vegetable glycerin prices have become more competitive and consumer preferences have shifted toward natural ingredients, many brands have switched away from propylene glycol even though it remains legal for use in dog products.

Typical Inclusion Rates and Cost Factors

In semi-moist dog foods and soft treats, propylene glycol typically shows up at about 5 to 12 percent by weight. This concentration provides enough moisture retention to keep products soft without making them sticky or overly wet. The compound works at lower inclusion rates than some alternatives because it's particularly effective at binding water molecules through its two hydroxyl groups. Going below 5 percent doesn't provide sufficient humectant action, while exceeding 12 percent can cause gastrointestinal upset in some dogs due to osmotic effects in the intestine.

Cost-wise, propylene glycol runs about $1.50 to $4.00 per kilogram for food-grade material, which made it attractive compared to vegetable glycerin's $2.00 to $5.00 per kilogram historical pricing. That cost advantage has largely disappeared as glycerin production has scaled up, removing one of the main economic justifications for using propylene glycol. The bigger issue now is consumer perception and the growing preference for natural ingredients over synthetic alternatives, even when both are considered safe by regulatory standards.

Safety Considerations and Industry Shift

Here's the uncomfortable truth about propylene glycol: it's completely banned in cat food because it causes Heinz body anemia in felines by damaging red blood cells. This happened enough times in the 1990s that the FDA prohibited its use in cat products entirely. Dogs metabolize propylene glycol differently through liver enzymes, converting it to lactic acid that gets processed through normal metabolic pathways. This is why it remains legal for dog food, but the cat toxicity creates reasonable concern among pet owners, especially in multi-pet households.

The safety margin for dogs sits at about 15 percent of body weight per day before you see adverse effects, which is way higher than typical dietary exposure from semi-moist foods. Still, some dogs show sensitivity at normal inclusion levels, experiencing loose stools or increased thirst. The lack of long-term feeding studies on chronic propylene glycol exposure in dogs leaves open questions about cumulative effects over years of consumption. Most veterinarians and pet nutritionists now recommend avoiding propylene glycol when alternatives exist, particularly for dogs with compromised liver or kidney function who might struggle with metabolizing and excreting the compound. The industry trend has been steadily moving away from propylene glycol toward vegetable glycerin, which performs the same function without the toxicity concerns or synthetic stigma.

How to Spot on Labels

Reading ingredient labels can be confusing. Here's how to identify and evaluate this ingredient:

What to Look For

  • Look for 'Propylene Glycol' in ingredient lists of semi-moist foods and soft treats
  • Typically appears in the first 5-10 ingredients due to relatively high inclusion levels (5-20%)
  • Common in chewy treats, semi-moist kibble, and "soft-moist" products
  • Rarely found in dry kibble or wet canned food

Alternative Names

This ingredient may also appear as:

  • Propylene glycol (most common name)
  • 1,2-propanediol (chemical name)
  • E1520 (European food additive code)
  • Propane-1,2-diol (alternative chemical name)

Red Flags

  • Propylene glycol in products marketed as "natural" or "holistic" (contradicts branding)
  • High position in ingredient list (suggests high percentage in formula)
  • Found in cat products (illegal and dangerous for cats)

Green Flags

  • Absence of propylene glycol in semi-moist products (uses vegetable glycerin or honey instead)
  • Dry kibble or wet canned food (doesn't require humectants)
  • Freeze-dried or dehydrated treats (moisture removed, no humectant needed)

Typical Position: Propylene glycol appears in the first 5-10 ingredients of semi-moist formulas since it comprises 5-20% of the product by weight. Its high position reflects its functional purpose as a moisture-retention agent, not necessarily a concern—but many owners prefer to avoid it anyway.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is propylene glycol still used if it's controversial?

Propylene Glycol is an effective, inexpensive preservative that prevents fat rancidity and extends shelf life. While approved by FDA and AAFCO, it remains controversial due to animal studies showing potential health concerns. Budget brands use it for cost savings. Many pet owners and premium manufacturers prefer natural preservatives like mixed tocopherols (vitamin E) and rosemary extract instead. If you're concerned, choose foods that explicitly list natural preservation.

What are safer alternatives to propylene glycol?

Natural preservatives like mixed tocopherols (vitamin E), rosemary extract, and ascorbic acid (vitamin C) are effective, well-tolerated alternatives. These work through antioxidant activity to prevent fat oxidation and rancidity. While slightly more expensive and sometimes requiring higher inclusion rates, they avoid the controversy surrounding synthetic preservatives. Look for foods that state 'preserved with mixed tocopherols' or 'preserved with natural antioxidants' on the label.

Should I avoid dog foods containing propylene glycol?

Propylene Glycol is rated 'Avoid' due to safety concerns. While approved by AAFCO, research suggests potential health risks. If you see this ingredient, consider it a red flag—look for brands using natural alternatives instead. It's not an immediate emergency if your current food contains it, but it's worth switching to a better formula.
