What It Is

Bean flour is ground dried beans (various types) used as a grain-free protein and carbohydrate source in pet foods.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. chickpea flour: Bean flour is a generic term for ground legumes (could be navy beans, black beans, etc.), while chickpea flour is specifically from garbanzo beans. Both boost plant protein.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include bean flour in dog food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Grain-free alternative to wheat flour
  • Provides plant protein
  • Gluten-free binding agent
  • Adds fiber and nutrients
  • Cost-effective grain-free option

Nutritional Profile

Macronutrients (varies by bean type)

  • Protein: 20-25% (plant-based, incomplete amino acid profile)
  • Fat: 1-3%
  • Moisture: 10-12% (flour form)
  • Carbohydrates: ~60% (includes starch and fiber)

Key Micronutrients

  • Fiber: Moderate to high (soluble and insoluble)
  • Iron: Good source (non-heme, lower bioavailability)
  • Folate: Present
  • Note: Legume-based protein; may contribute to DCM risk if primary protein source (under investigation)

Quality Considerations

When evaluating bean flour in dog products, it's important to understand protein density, amino acid profile, digestibility, and sourcing quality. This ingredient's quality and appropriateness can vary significantly based on sourcing, processing, and the specific formula it's used in.

Quality Note

Bean flour can be made from various beans (navy, pinto, black, etc.) or a mixture. It provides moderate plant protein and fiber along with some micronutrients. The quality varies depending on the bean source. Like all legume flours, it's more nutritious than grain flours but contains anti-nutrients and incomplete protein. The vague name 'bean flour' without specifying the bean type is less transparent than named bean flours.

How to Spot on Labels

Reading ingredient labels can be confusing. Here's how to identify and evaluate this ingredient:

What to Look For

  • Look for 'Bean Flour' (generic) - positions vary but often 5-12 in grain-free formulas
  • Prefer specific bean types over generic 'bean flour': chickpea flour, lentil flour, navy bean flour are more transparent
  • Check for legume stacking: bean flour + peas + lentils + chickpeas in top ingredients signals plant protein overuse
  • Ensure animal proteins (chicken, fish, beef meals) appear multiple times before bean flour
  • Guaranteed analysis should show 25-35% protein if animal proteins are truly primary

Alternative Names

This ingredient may also appear as:

  • Legume flour (broader category)
  • Pulse flour (technical term for legume flours)
  • Specific types: chickpea flour, lentil flour, pea flour, navy bean flour, black bean flour
  • Garbanzo bean flour (chickpeas)

Red Flags

  • Generic 'bean flour' without species specification (transparency issue)
  • Appears in top 3 ingredients before sufficient animal protein
  • Multiple legume ingredients stacked: bean flour + peas + pea protein + lentils (plant protein overload)
  • Used in grain-free formula with limited animal protein (potential DCM concern)
  • No indication of bean type or processing method

Green Flags

  • Specific bean type identified (chickpea flour, lentil flour vs generic bean flour)
  • Appears after 2-3 named animal protein sources
  • Part of diverse carbohydrate base (sweet potato + bean flour + oats) rather than dominant
  • Formula includes taurine and L-carnitine supplementation (mitigates potential DCM concerns)
  • Organic certification specified

Typical Position: Bean flour typically appears in positions 5-10 in grain-free formulas, after animal proteins and fats but before supplemental ingredients. Earlier positioning (1-4) signals overreliance on plant proteins.

Watts' Take

Bean flour is an acceptable grain-free ingredient, though we prefer when manufacturers specify which beans (e.g., 'chickpea flour' vs. generic 'bean flour'). It's better than corn or wheat flour but shouldn't be a primary protein contributor. We appreciate its use in grain-free formulas as long as animal proteins remain primary. The lack of specificity (which beans?) is slightly concerning but not a deal-breaker.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where should bean flour appear on the ingredient list?

Bean flour typically appears in positions 5-10 in grain-free formulas, after named animal proteins and fats. If bean flour appears in positions 1-4 before adequate meat sources, the food relies too heavily on plant protein. Watch for legume stacking—bean flour plus peas plus lentils plus chickpeas in the top ingredients signals excessive plant protein, which has been investigated for potential links to dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in dogs.

Is bean flour necessary in dog food?

No, dogs don't need bean flour. It's primarily used in grain-free formulas as a carbohydrate and protein source to replace grains. While beans provide fiber and some plant protein, dogs require animal-based protein for optimal amino acid profiles. Bean flour is a functional ingredient for creating grain-free kibble texture, not a nutritional necessity.

How is bean flour processed for dog food?

Bean flour is made by drying beans and grinding them into powder. The generic term 'bean flour' without specifying the type (navy, pinto, black beans) is a transparency concern—premium brands specify which beans are used. Proper processing reduces antinutrients like lectins and phytates that can interfere with nutrient absorption, but even processed bean flour contains some antinutritional factors.
