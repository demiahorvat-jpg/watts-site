What Is Herring Meal?

Herring meal is a concentrated protein ingredient made by cooking and drying herring fish (Clupea harengus), then grinding into protein-rich powder. Herring meal contains approximately 70-75% protein, 8-12% fat (including beneficial omega-3 fatty acids EPA and DHA), 5-10% moisture, and 15-20% ash. As rendered meal, herring meal provides 3-4 times more protein per weight than fresh herring (which is 70-75% water). Herring are small, oily fish rich in omega-3s—herring meal provides exceptional EPA and DHA for heart health, brain function, joint health, and coat quality. Herring are lower on food chain than large fish like tuna, resulting in lower mercury accumulation. Herring meal is premium fish ingredient signaling quality marine protein. More expensive than generic 'fish meal' but provides superior omega-3 profile and traceable protein source.

Why Manufacturers Add Herring Meal to Dog Food

Herring meal appears in dog food for concentrated marine protein (70-75%), complete amino acid profile, and exceptional omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA). It provides 3-4x more protein than fresh herring due to water removal. Herring's omega-3s support heart health, cognitive function, joint health, anti-inflammatory benefits, and coat quality. Named fish meal signals quality and traceability. Herring are sustainable, lower mercury than large fish, and nutrient-dense. Premium brands use herring meal for superior nutrition and omega-3 benefits.

How Herring Meal Is Made & Used

From Whole Fish to Concentrated Meal

Herring meal production follows a well-established rendering process that transforms whole herring into a shelf-stable, protein-dense ingredient. The process starts with whole herring, typically caught in the North Atlantic or Pacific oceans. These fish get cooked at temperatures around 90-100°C for several hours, which accomplishes several things at once - it kills any bacteria or parasites, breaks down cell walls to release oils, and denatures proteins for better digestibility. After cooking, the material passes through screw presses that squeeze out most of the oil, which gets sold separately as herring oil for supplements.

What's left after pressing is a solid cake containing about 65-70% protein along with minerals from bones and scales. This cake gets dried using heated air or steam-heated dryers to reduce moisture content to about 10% or less, preventing spoilage and creating shelf stability. Finally, the dried material gets ground into a fine powder that's easy to mix into dog food formulas. The entire process typically takes 6-8 hours from whole fish to finished meal. Quality manufacturers pay close attention to temperatures throughout, as excessive heat can damage amino acids and reduce protein quality.

Protein and Omega-3 Profiles

Herring meal typically delivers 65-72% protein on a dry matter basis, putting it among the most concentrated protein sources available for dog food. The exact percentage depends on how thoroughly the oil was pressed out - more aggressive pressing means more oil removed and therefore higher protein concentration. The remaining fat content usually sits around 8-12%, which still provides substantial omega-3 fatty acids even after the bulk of the oil has been extracted.

The omega-3 profile is particularly impressive. Herring meal typically contains about 1.5-2.5% combined EPA and DHA even after oil extraction, with the ratio usually favoring EPA slightly. This is significantly higher than most land animal proteins and even exceeds many other fish meals. The amino acid profile is equally strong, with high levels of lysine, methionine, and threonine that complement plant proteins nicely. Digestibility typically ranges from 85-90%, meaning dogs can actually use most of the protein provided.

Cost and Inclusion Rates

Herring meal costs about $2.00-3.50 per kilogram in bulk, making it a premium fish meal option. It's more expensive than generic fish meal but typically less costly than salmon meal, positioning it as a "value premium" ingredient. The price varies based on fishing season success, processing quality, and global demand from both pet food and aquaculture industries. North Atlantic herring fisheries produce most of the supply, with Norway, Iceland, and Canada being major sources.

Dog food manufacturers typically include herring meal at rates of 8-20% in fish-based formulas. At 10% inclusion in a formula, herring meal contributes about 6.5-7% protein to the final food, making it a substantial protein source. Premium fish-based dog foods might use 15-20% herring meal as a primary protein, while mid-tier brands might use 8-12% combined with other proteins. The key is that even moderate inclusion rates provide meaningful protein and omega-3 benefits without driving costs too high.

Quality Variations and Market Factors

Not all herring meal is created equal. Quality grades exist based on protein content, freshness at processing, and manufacturing methods. "Prime" or "high-grade" herring meal contains 68% or more protein and comes from fresh fish processed quickly after catch. "Standard" grade might run 62-67% protein and could include fish that were stored longer before processing. The freshness at processing makes a big difference - fish that sit too long before rendering can develop oxidation and off-flavors that reduce palatability and nutritional value.

Sustainability is another quality factor gaining importance. Many North Atlantic herring fisheries carry MSC certification for sustainable fishing practices, but not all do. Herring populations can fluctuate based on ocean temperatures, fishing pressure, and ecosystem health, so sourcing from well-managed fisheries matters for long-term availability. Pet food brands increasingly specify "sustainably sourced herring meal" to appeal to environmentally conscious consumers and ensure reliable supply chains. When you see herring meal on a label, the quality and sustainability depend heavily on the manufacturer's sourcing standards, which unfortunately aren't always transparent.

Finding Herring Meal on Pet Food Labels

Herring meal appears as "herring meal," "menhaden herring meal," or occasionally "fish meal (herring)" if specified further.

Alternative Names

  • Menhaden herring meal
  • Herring fish meal
  • Dried herring

Positioning on Labels

Herring meal is rendered and concentrated (60-65% protein), so it appears mid-list even when providing substantial protein. Common in positions 3-7 in fish-based formulas. If herring meal is #1 ingredient, the formula is fish-heavy.

Red Flags

  • Generic "fish meal" without species specified instead of "herring meal" = lower transparency
  • Herring meal paired with low-quality proteins (meat meal, poultry by-product meal) = mixed quality signals

Green Flags

  • Herring meal as primary protein (top 3 ingredients)
  • Paired with other named fish proteins (salmon, whitefish, mackerel)
  • Listed with fish oil for complete omega-3 profile
  • "Sustainably sourced herring meal" or sustainability certifications

Quality Indicators

Herring meal is premium ingredient. Wild-caught cold-water fish, rich in omega-3s, and high protein quality. Its presence signals quality formulation. Look for foods pairing herring meal with fish oil to maximize omega-3 benefits. Excellent for dogs needing anti-inflammatory support (skin allergies, arthritis) or novel protein diets (fish is less common allergen than chicken or beef).

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes herring meal different from generic 'fish meal'?

Herring meal specifies the exact fish species (Clupea harengus), while generic 'fish meal' can be any combination of fish species with unknown quality and origin. Named fish meals like herring meal provide traceability, consistent omega-3 profiles, and quality assurance. Generic fish meal could be premium fish or low-grade processing byproducts—you simply don't know. Always prefer named fish meals (herring, salmon, whitefish, anchovy) over unspecified 'fish meal.'

Should I worry about mercury in herring meal?

No. Herring are small, short-lived fish low on the food chain, which means they don't accumulate significant mercury or other heavy metals. Large predatory fish like tuna, shark, and swordfish bioaccumulate mercury from eating smaller fish throughout their longer lives. Herring and similar small fish (anchovies, sardines, mackerel) are among the safest fish for regular consumption precisely because of their position in the food chain.

Does herring meal still provide omega-3s after rendering?

Yes, though less than fresh herring. Rendering removes most oil (sold separately as fish oil), but herring meal typically retains 8-12% fat with 1.5-2.5% combined EPA and DHA. This is still significantly higher than land animal proteins. For maximum omega-3 benefits, look for foods that combine herring meal with added fish oil—the meal provides concentrated protein while the oil boosts omega-3 content.
