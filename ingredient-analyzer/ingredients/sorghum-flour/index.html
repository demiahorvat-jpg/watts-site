What It Is

Sorghum flour is ground grain from sorghum (Sorghum bicolor), used as a gluten-free grain alternative in pet foods.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. rice flour: Both are gluten-free grain flours. Sorghum flour is higher in protein and fiber with a mild flavor, while rice flour is blander, more easily digested, and more common in sensitive stomach formulas.
  • vs. oat flour: Both are grain flours. Sorghum flour is gluten-free with more resistant starch, while oat flour contains beta-glucans for heart health but may contain trace gluten.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include sorghum flour in dog food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Gluten-free grain alternative
  • Provides complex carbohydrates for energy
  • Contains B vitamins and minerals
  • Lower glycemic index than corn
  • Hypoallergenic grain option

Quality Considerations

When evaluating sorghum flour in dog products, it's important to understand protein density, amino acid profile, digestibility, and sourcing quality. This ingredient's quality and appropriateness can vary significantly based on sourcing, processing, and the specific formula it's used in.

Quality Note

Sorghum flour is a whole grain flour offering moderate nutritional value. It's gluten-free and contains more protein and fiber than refined grains like white rice. It provides B vitamins, magnesium, and antioxidants. However, it's still primarily a starchy carbohydrate source with limited protein quality. It's better than corn or wheat for dogs with grain sensitivities, but it's not particularly nutrient-dense. A decent grain option but nothing exceptional.

Scientific Evidence

Sorghum flour is produced by grinding whole sorghum grain into a fine powder. Sorghum is an ancient, gluten-free grain increasingly used in grain-inclusive and 'ancient grain' dog food formulas.

Key Research Findings

  • Nutritional Profile: Sorghum flour contains 8-12% protein with incomplete amino acids for dogs, 3-4% fat, 3-5% fiber, and approximately 70-75% carbohydrates. It provides B vitamins (niacin, thiamine), iron, phosphorus, and antioxidants.
  • Antioxidant Content: Sorghum contains phenolic compounds and tannins with antioxidant properties. However, tannins can also reduce protein and mineral digestibility if present in high concentrations.
  • Digestibility: Sorghum digestibility in dogs ranges from 75-85%, lower than rice or oats but comparable to corn. Tannin content in some varieties further reduces digestibility.
  • Glycemic Response: Sorghum has a moderate glycemic index (60-70), lower than white rice or corn due to its fiber and resistant starch content. This makes it suitable for stable blood sugar management.
  • Gluten-Free: Sorghum is naturally gluten-free and suitable for dogs with grain sensitivities, though true grain allergies in dogs are relatively rare.
  • Sustainability: Sorghum is drought-resistant and requires less water than corn or wheat, making it an environmentally sustainable grain choice.

Evidence Level: Moderate - Sorghum is less studied in pet nutrition than rice or oats, but available research supports its nutritional adequacy as a grain source for dogs.

How to Spot on Labels

Sorghum flour appears in ancient grain and grain-inclusive formulas marketed for natural, whole-food nutrition.

What to Look For

  • Typically appears in positions 4-10 in ancient grain or whole grain formulas
  • Often paired with other ancient grains (millet, quinoa, oats, barley)
  • Look for 'whole grain sorghum flour' for maximum fiber and nutrient retention
  • Check protein levels: 26%+ suggests adequate animal protein despite grain inclusion

Alternative Names

This ingredient may also appear as:

  • Whole grain sorghum flour
  • Ground sorghum
  • Sorghum meal
  • Milo flour (sorghum is also called milo)

Red Flags

  • Listed in top 3 ingredients ahead of meat meals
  • Appears alongside multiple other grain flours creating high carb load
  • Generic 'sorghum flour' without 'whole grain' specification
  • Present in formulas with under 24% protein (grain-heavy recipe)

Green Flags

  • Specified as 'whole grain sorghum flour' or 'organic sorghum'
  • Appears after multiple animal protein sources in ingredient list
  • Part of a diverse ancient grain blend with varied nutrition
  • Used in formulas emphasizing sustainability or novel grains
  • Paired with 28%+ protein ensuring adequate meat content

Typical Position: Positions 4-10 in ancient grain formulas; should follow meat meals in quality foods.

Watts' Take

Sorghum flour is an acceptable grain alternative - better than corn or wheat, especially for dogs with sensitivities. It's a whole grain with some nutritional benefits. However, it's still primarily starch and doesn't offer the nutrient density of animal proteins or organ meats. We view it as a reasonable carbohydrate source in grain-inclusive formulas, though we'd still prefer oats or brown rice. It's neutral - not a red flag, not a selling point.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is sorghum flour grain-free?

No, sorghum flour is a grain and a complex carbohydrate source. Grains don't automatically mean healthier—what matters is digestibility and nutritional value. Sorghum Flour provides digestible energy and is acceptable in properly formulated dog food.

Is sorghum flour easier to digest than other carbohydrates?

Digestibility varies by individual dog and the processing method. Sorghum Flour has moderate digestibility for most dogs. Dogs with grain sensitivities may do better with alternative carbohydrate sources, but true grain allergies are relatively rare. If your dog tolerates sorghum flour well, there's no need to avoid it.

Where should sorghum flour appear on the ingredient list?

Sorghum flour typically appears in positions 4-10 in ancient grain or grain-inclusive formulas. It should follow meat meals and named animal proteins—not precede them. If sorghum flour appears in the top 3 ingredients ahead of meat sources, the formula may be carbohydrate-heavy. Positioning in the 5-8 range after quality animal proteins indicates appropriate use as a carbohydrate source. Look for formulas with 26%+ protein to ensure adequate meat despite grain inclusion.
