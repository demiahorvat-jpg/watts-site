Quick Summary

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is essential for calcium absorption in dogs and cats. Unlike humans, neither species can make vitamin D from sunlight—they must get it from food. Cats especially need D3 rather than D2, which they metabolize poorly.

What It Is

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is a fat-soluble vitamin essential for calcium and phosphorus absorption. Unlike humans, dogs and cats have very limited ability to synthesize vitamin D from sunlight—both species lack sufficient 7-dehydrocholesterol in their skin. This makes dietary vitamin D essential for all dogs and cats, regardless of sun exposure.

For cats: D3 is especially critical because cats discriminate against vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol), metabolizing it very poorly. Cats must have D3 from animal sources.

For dogs: D3 is 3-10x more effective than D2, though dogs can use D2 in a pinch. Quality foods use D3.

  • vs. vitamin D2 supplement: D2 (ergocalciferol) is plant-derived, D3 is animal-derived. D3 is 3-10x more effective in dogs. Cats especially cannot use D2 efficiently—D3 is critical for cats.
  • vs. calcium carbonate: Vitamin D3 helps absorb calcium; calcium carbonate provides the calcium itself. Both needed together for bone health in dogs and cats.

Why It's Used in Pet Food

Vitamin D3 is added to dog and cat foods because:

  • Neither species can make enough vitamin D from sunlight
  • Essential for calcium and phosphorus absorption
  • Required for bone health and immune function
  • Processing destroys natural vitamin D in ingredients

Nutritional Profile

AAFCO Requirements

  • For dogs: Minimum 500 IU/kg, maximum 3,000 IU/kg (reduced from 5,000 in 2016)
  • For cats: Minimum 280 IU/kg, maximum 30,080 IU/kg (higher tolerance than dogs)

D3 vs D2 Effectiveness

  • For dogs: D3 is 3-10x more biologically active than D2
  • For cats: D3 is critical—cats discriminate against D2 and metabolize it very poorly

Quality Considerations

For cats: Ensure the food specifies D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2. Cats cannot efficiently use plant-derived D2.

For dogs: D3 is preferred but dogs can use D2 if necessary. Look for D3 specification in quality foods.

Toxicity: Vitamin D toxicity causes hypercalcemia (elevated blood calcium), leading to kidney damage and soft tissue calcification. Dogs are more susceptible—the 2016 AAFCO maximum was reduced from 5,000 to 3,000 IU/kg after toxicity concerns.

Scientific Evidence

Why Pets Need Dietary Vitamin D

Unlike humans, dogs and cats have very limited 7-dehydrocholesterol in their skin—the precursor that converts to vitamin D3 when exposed to UV light. Even hairless dogs and cats make insufficient amounts. Both species are entirely dependent on dietary vitamin D.

D3 vs D2 Research

For dogs: D3 is 3-10x more biologically active than D2. Dogs metabolize D3 to 25-hydroxyvitamin D3 far more efficiently, maintaining vitamin D status much longer.

For cats: Cats discriminate against D2 (ergocalciferol), using it at a much less efficient rate than D3. This makes D3 critical for cats—they cannot rely on plant-derived D2.

Toxicity

For dogs: 0.1 mg/kg cholecalciferol causes GI signs; 0.5 mg/kg causes hypercalcemia and kidney injury. AAFCO maximum is 3,000 IU/kg.

For cats: Suspected to be more susceptible to toxicity, but no established toxic dose. AAFCO allows higher maximum (30,080 IU/kg) based on different metabolism.

Practical Insights

Natural vs Synthetic D3

Most D3 is extracted from lanolin (sheep wool oil) via UV irradiation—the same process that occurs in skin. Synthetic D3 is chemically identical and equally effective. Both forms are well-absorbed when consumed with dietary fat.

Why D3 Over D2

D2 (ergocalciferol) comes from plant sources and costs less, but D3 is far more effective. Quality pet foods use D3. D2 appears mainly in budget formulas or vegan pet foods—problematic for cats who cannot use D2 efficiently.

Recalls and Safety

Manufacturing errors have occasionally caused vitamin D over-fortification and recalls. Quality manufacturers batch-test vitamin D levels. Look for brands with guaranteed analysis showing D3 levels.

Label Guidance

Names to Look For

Vitamin D3 supplement, cholecalciferol, vitamin D3. Avoid generic "vitamin D supplement" without the "3"—may indicate D2.

What to Check

  • For cats: Must be D3 (cholecalciferol). Cats cannot efficiently use D2.
  • For dogs: D3 preferred but D2 acceptable. Look for D3 specification in quality foods.
  • Levels: 500-2,000 IU/kg is typical. Above 3,000 IU/kg approaches AAFCO maximum for dogs.
Watts' Take

Essential for both dogs and cats—neither species can make vitamin D from sunlight. D3 is the preferred form, and it's critical for cats who cannot efficiently use D2. All quality complete foods include D3 supplementation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why can't dogs and cats make vitamin D from sunlight?

Unlike humans, dogs and cats have very limited amounts of 7-dehydrocholesterol in their skin—the precursor that converts to vitamin D3 when exposed to UV light. Fur also blocks UV rays. This makes both species entirely dependent on dietary vitamin D, regardless of time spent outdoors.

What's the difference between vitamin D3 and D2 for pets?

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is 3-10 times more biologically active than D2 (ergocalciferol) in dogs. Cats especially cannot use D2 efficiently—they discriminate against it metabolically, making D3 critical for cats. Quality pet foods specify D3 rather than generic "vitamin D."

Why is D3 especially important for cats?

Cats discriminate against vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol), metabolizing it very poorly. This makes D3 (cholecalciferol) critical for cats—they cannot rely on plant-derived D2. All quality cat foods use D3, but this is especially important for cats on homemade or vegan diets.
