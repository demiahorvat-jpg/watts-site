What It Is

Navy beans are small white beans used in pet foods as a source of plant protein, fiber, and complex carbohydrates.

Compare to Similar Ingredients

  • vs. chickpeas: Navy beans have slightly more fiber and folate, while chickpeas have more protein and are better tolerated. Both are grain-free legumes providing moderate plant protein. Similar nutritional profiles.
  • vs. lentils: Lentils have more protein (25% vs 21%) and iron than navy beans. Navy beans have more fiber. Both are quality legumes, but lentils are nutritionally denser and more commonly used in dog food.
  • vs. peas: Peas and navy beans are both legumes providing plant protein and fiber. Peas are more common, digestible, and better tolerated. Navy beans have more fiber but may cause more gas.
  • vs. chicken: Chicken is complete animal protein with superior amino acid profile and bioavailability. Navy beans are incomplete plant protein requiring complementation. Animal proteins are vastly superior to legume proteins for dogs.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include navy beans in dog food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Plant-based protein source
  • Rich in dietary fiber
  • Contains iron, magnesium, and B vitamins
  • Grain-free carbohydrate option
  • Supports digestive health

Quality Considerations

When evaluating navy beans in dog products, it's important to understand protein density, amino acid profile, digestibility, and sourcing quality. This ingredient's quality and appropriateness can vary significantly based on sourcing, processing, and the specific formula it's used in.

Quality Note

Navy beans are nutrient-dense legumes offering moderate plant protein (about 21g per 100g cooked), significant fiber, and micronutrients. They're particularly high in folate and manganese. Like all beans, they contain anti-nutrients that can reduce mineral absorption and may cause gas in some dogs. The protein is incomplete for canine nutrition. They're a reasonable grain-free ingredient but should complement animal proteins, not replace them.

Scientific Evidence

Nutritional Profile

Navy beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) are small white legumes rich in protein (20-25%), complex carbohydrates (60-65%), and dietary fiber (15-20%) on a dry matter basis. They provide minerals including iron, magnesium, potassium, and zinc, as well as B vitamins, particularly folate. The nutrient density makes navy beans a valuable ingredient in plant-based protein formulations.

Protein Quality

Navy bean protein is incomplete for dogs, lacking adequate levels of methionine and tryptophan compared to animal proteins. However, when combined with grains or animal proteins, navy beans can contribute meaningfully to overall protein content. The biological value is lower than animal proteins, with digestibility ranging from 70-85% depending on processing.

Resistant Starch and Fiber

Navy beans contain significant amounts of resistant starch and both soluble and insoluble fiber. The fiber provides prebiotic benefits, supporting beneficial gut bacteria and producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) during fermentation. This can support colon health and overall digestive function.

Anti-Nutritional Factors

Raw navy beans contain anti-nutritional factors including lectins (phytohemagglutinin), trypsin inhibitors, and phytic acid. These compounds can reduce nutrient absorption and cause digestive upset. Proper cooking and processing (heat treatment during kibble production) significantly reduces these factors, making cooked navy beans safe and digestible for dogs.

DCM and Legume Concerns

Navy beans are a pulse legume, and the FDA has been investigating a potential link between grain-free diets high in peas, lentils, and other legumes with dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in dogs. While the exact mechanism remains unclear, formulas heavily reliant on legumes as primary ingredients may carry potential risk. Moderate inclusion as a supplemental ingredient appears less concerning.

Evidence Rating: Moderate

Navy beans are nutritious legumes with clear benefits when properly processed. Concerns exist regarding heavy legume use in grain-free diets, but moderate inclusion is well-supported by nutritional evidence.

Label Guidance

Common Names on Labels

  • Navy Beans
  • White Beans
  • Navy Bean
  • Dried Navy Beans

Label Positioning

Navy beans typically appear in the middle portion of ingredient lists (positions 8-15) in formulas using them as supplemental protein and fiber. In grain-free or high-legume formulas, they may appear higher. Inclusion rates generally range from 3-10% in standard recipes.

Green Flags

  • Protein and fiber source: Provides both macronutrients in significant amounts
  • Nutrient density: Rich in minerals and B vitamins
  • Lower list positioning: When used as supplemental ingredient after animal proteins
  • Variety in legumes: When part of diverse ingredient mix vs. sole legume source

Red Flags

  • High positioning in grain-free: May contribute to legume-heavy formulation
  • Multiple legume sources: When combined with peas, lentils, chickpeas, etc.
  • Replaces animal protein: When navy beans rank higher than secondary meat sources
  • Primary protein source: Dogs require animal protein for complete amino acid profile

Quality Indicators

Navy beans are a moderate-quality ingredient—nutritious when used appropriately but concerning when overused. Look for formulas where animal proteins dominate the first 5 ingredients, with navy beans appearing later as a supplemental component. Avoid formulas where navy beans plus other legumes collectively outweigh animal protein sources.

Watts' Take

Navy beans are acceptable whole food legumes that add protein and fiber to grain-free formulas. They're better than cheap grain fillers and provide legitimate nutrients. However, we always prioritize animal proteins. Beans should be minor ingredients, not foundations of the diet. If navy beans appear high on the list alongside other legumes, it's a sign of too much plant protein. In moderation, they're fine.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are navy beans good for dogs?

Navy beans provide plant-based protein, fiber, and nutrients like folate and manganese. They're a quality carbohydrate source often used in grain-free formulas. When properly cooked, they're digestible and nutritious. However, they shouldn't replace animal protein as dogs' primary protein source.

Can dogs digest beans properly?

Dogs can digest properly cooked beans, though not as efficiently as meat. Cooking breaks down compounds that can cause digestive issues. Some dogs may experience gas when eating beans, especially initially. The fiber in beans can actually support digestive health when introduced gradually.

Are navy beans part of the DCM controversy?

Legumes including navy beans were mentioned in FDA reports investigating grain-free diets and dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM). The relationship isn't fully understood, and legumes themselves may not be the cause. If concerned, choose foods where legumes aren't among the top ingredients or consult your veterinarian.
