What It Is

Non-rendered parts from slaughtered animals—organs, blood, bone, fatty tissue. Excludes meat, hide, hooves, hair, horns.

Why It's Used in Dog Products

Manufacturers include meat by-products in dog food, treats, and supplements for several reasons:

  • Cheap protein source using slaughter waste
  • Can include nutrient-rich organs (liver, kidney)
  • Highly variable quality and composition

Key Considerations

When evaluating meat by-products in dog products, it's important to understand protein density, amino acid profile, digestibility, and sourcing quality. This ingredient's quality and appropriateness can vary significantly based on sourcing, processing, and the specific formula it's used in.

Quality Note

Extremely variable quality. Can range from nutrient-rich organs to low-quality waste. No transparency on source or quality.

Potential Concerns

While meat by-products can be appropriate in dog nutrition, pet owners should be aware of allergies, protein digestibility, and quality variations between named and generic sources. Individual dogs may respond differently to the same ingredient based on their health status, age, and sensitivities.

Watts' Take

We avoid by-products due to lack of transparency and quality consistency. While organs are nutritious, 'by-products' doesn't guarantee you're getting liver vs low-value waste.

Can meat by-products cause allergies in dogs?

Any protein source can potentially trigger allergies in sensitive dogs. Signs of food allergies include itchy skin, ear infections, digestive issues, and excessive paw licking. If your dog shows these symptoms, consult your veterinarian about an elimination diet to identify the trigger ingredient.

How much meat by-products should be in dog food?

Quality dog foods typically list primary protein sources like meat by-products within the first 3-5 ingredients. The exact percentage varies by formula, but protein-focused foods generally contain 25-35% total protein for adults, and 28-40% for puppies or performance dogs.
